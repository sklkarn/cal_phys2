    Section 3.8 Examples C

    Electromagnetic Induction.

    Example 3.8.1.

    A coil of wire of radius 5 cm and 100 turns is centered at the origin and lies in the x/y plane. A uniform magnetic field is in the -z direction and is increasing at the rate of 0.2 T/s. If the resistance of the coil is \(50 \Omega,\) find
    1. the emf induced in the coil,
    2. the current in the coil,
    3. the direction of the current in the coil,
    4. the direction of the magnetic field resulting from the current in the coil,
    5. the power required to keep the original magnetic field increasing at 0.2 T/s.
    Solution.
    Given: \(r=0.05\,m, \quad N=100, \quad B=(0.2\,T/s)t,\) \(R = 50\,\Omega\)
    1. \begin{equation*} \Phi_{B} = \int \vec{B}\cdot \vec{\,dA} = BA \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} A=\pi r^{2} = 0.00785 \,m^{2} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \varepsilon = -N\frac{\,d\Phi_{B}}{\,dt} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \Phi_{B} = (0.00157 \,W_{b}/s)t \end{equation*}
    2. \begin{equation*} \varepsilon = IR; \quad I = -3.14 \,mA \end{equation*}
    3. Counter Clockwise
    4. +z direction (opposite increasing B).
    5. \begin{equation*} P = I\varepsilon =I^{2}R = 0.493 \,mW. \end{equation*}

    Example 3.8.2.

    A two parallel long straight wires separated by a distance L = 30 cm are connected by a third straight wire on their left end. A sliding bar, also of length L, is placed across the two long wires and slid to the right with a constant speed \(v = 2 \,m/s\) in the +x direction. Everything is placed in a uniform magnetic field B = 10 T directed into the paper as shown in figure below.
    If the bar has a resistance of \(10 \,\Omega\) and the other wires have negligible resistance, find magnitude and direction of the
    1. emf induced across the bar,
    2. current in the bar,
    3. magnetic force on the bar.
    4. How much power is required to pull the bar through the magnetic field?
    5. How much power is dissipated in the bar in the form of heat?
    Solution.
    Given: \(L=0.3\,m, \quad v=2\,m/s, \quad B=10\,T, \quad R = 10\,\Omega \)
    1. \begin{equation*} \varepsilon = -\frac{\,d\Phi_{B}}{\,dt}; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \Phi_{B} = \int\vec{B}\cdot\vec{\,dA}=BA; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} A = L x;\quad x = vt \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \varepsilon = 6 \,V \quad \text{up} \end{equation*}
    2. \begin{equation*} \epsilon = I R \Rightarrow I = 0.6 \,A \quad \text{up} \end{equation*}
    3. \begin{equation*} F = I L B = 1.8 \,N \quad \text{left} \end{equation*}
    4. \begin{equation*} P = F v = 3.6 \,W \end{equation*}
    5. \begin{equation*} P = I\varepsilon =I^{2}R = 3.6 \,W \end{equation*}

    Example 3.8.3.

    A conducting wire form a semicircle ab of a radius r. The wire has negligible resistance. A resister R connects an end of the wire, \(b\) to the center of the circle, \(c\) as shown in figure. A conducting rod connects the center, \(c\) to the semicircle wire and sweeps around at an angular speed \(\omega\) in the counterclockwise direction (see Figure). The rod has negligible resistance. A uniform magnetic field B perpendicular to the plane of the circle (into the plane). Find change in the magnetic flux during small interval of time t, Find the current induced in the loop, and is the direction of the induced current clockwise or counterclockwise?.
    Solution.
    The area of a circle can be determined by
    \begin{equation*} \,dA = \text{area of shaded region} = \frac{1}{2}r\,ds \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = \frac{1}{2}r (r\,d\theta) =\frac{1}{2}r^{2} \,d\theta \end{equation*}
    Therefore change in magnetic flux,
    \begin{equation*} \varepsilon = emf= -\frac{\,d\Phi}{\,dt} = -B\left(\frac{-\,dA}{\,dt}\right) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\frac{1}{2}r^{2}B\left(\frac{\,d\theta}{\,dt}\right) = \frac{1}{2}r^{2}B \omega \end{equation*}
    Current induced in the loop is
    \begin{equation*} I=\frac{emf}{R} \end{equation*}
    The direction of current is clockwise as the loop opposes the flux to be decreased hence current is induced in a circuit to substitute the decreasing flux. Use Fleming‚Äôs right hand rule to determine the direction of induced current.

    Example 3.8.4.

    A rectangular loop of length, l width, w is placed at a distance, h from a current carrying wire whose current is varying as
    \begin{equation*} I=a+bt \end{equation*}
    where a and b are constants. If b = 10 A/s, h = 1 cm, w = 10 cm, and l = 100 cm, find the magnetic flux, induced emf, and the direction of induced current in the loop.
    Solution.
    Magnetic field at a distance, x from current carrying conductor is given by
    \begin{equation*} B= \frac{\mu_{o}I}{2\pi x}. \end{equation*}
    Now magnetic flux through a differential area \(\,dA \) is given by
    \begin{equation*} \,d\Phi=\vec{B}\cdot\vec{\,dA} = B\,dA = BL\,dx \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad \Phi = \int\,d\Phi= \int\limits_{h}^{h+w} \frac{\mu_{o}IL\,dx}{2\pi x} = \left.\frac{\mu_{o}IL}{2\pi} \ln x\right]_{h}^{h+w} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = \frac{\mu_{o}IL}{2\pi} ln\left(\frac{h+w}{h}\right) = \frac{\mu_{o}L}{2\pi} (a+bt) ln\left(\frac{h+w}{h}\right) \end{equation*}
    Magnetic field is in decreasing order as point P shifts away form the current carrying conductor but flux increases on every layer in a loop with time as current increases in a wire.
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad \varepsilon = - \frac{\,d\Phi}{\,dt} = - \frac{\mu_{o}L}{2\pi}ln\left(\frac{h+w}{h}\right)b \end{equation*}
    As the current changes in a wire, it changes the magnetic flux into the loop. Hence from Lenz‚Äôs law the direction of induced current in a loop must opposes this change, meaning the direction of current must be counterclockwise if current increases in a wire.

    Example 3.8.5.

    A conducting loop of mass, M resistance, R length, l and width, w is falling into magnetic field, B pointing into the page. Find the terminal velocity \(v_{t}\) attained by the loop before its top edge enters into the field.
    Solution.
    When lower arm of the loop enters into external magnetic field flux starts increasing in the loop and hence loop induces its current in such a way that it could oppose that change. Consequently, loop induced magnetic field, \(\vec{B}_{ind} \) just opposite to external magnetic field, \(\vec{B} \text{.}\) Meaning if external field \(\vec{B}\) is pointed into the page then induced field \(\vec{B}_{ind}\) by the loop is pointed out of the page. Now from Right Hand Thumb rule the induced current, I in the loop must be counterclockwise. As current, I is flowing in the conductor of width, w inside the external field \(\vec{B}\text{,}\) it starts experiencing a magetic force \(\vec{F}_{b}\) upwards which can be found by Fleming‚Äôs Left Hand Rule: Forefinger along \(\vec{B} \text{,}\) middlefinger along currenet I, then thumb gives the direction of force \(\vec{F}_{b} \) upward. Now, emf induced in the loop is given by
    \begin{equation*} e = -\frac{\,d\phi}{\,dt} = -B l \frac{\,dy}{\,dt} = -\,B \,l\, v \end{equation*}
    As B and w are constant and loop is falling into the field along width w where we have considered \(\,dy\) as small elementry width of the loop. Since R is the resistance of the loop, we have magnitude of emf, \(e = IR.\)
    \begin{equation} \therefore\quad IR = Blv \tag{3.8.1} \end{equation}
    \begin{equation} also, \quad F_{b} = BIl \tag{3.8.2} \end{equation}
    Using eqns. (3.8.1) and (3.8.2), we have-
    \begin{equation} F_{b} = \frac{B^{2}l^{2}v }{R} \tag{3.8.3} \end{equation}
    Note that the magnetic force on the left and right arms of the loop cancell out each other and there is no magnetic force acting on the top arm. Now before sufficient portion of the loop falls into the magnetic field the magnetidue of \(F_{b}\) keeps increasing and finally gets balanced by the \(F_{g}. \) At this point \(v\) becomes constant called terminal velocity, \(v_{t}.\) Hence,
    \begin{equation*} F_{b} = F_{g} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or, \quad \frac{B^{2}l^{2}v_{t} }{R} = mg \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad v_{t} = \frac{mgR}{B^{2}l^{2}} \end{equation*}
    Here, \(v_{t}\) proportional to R and inversely proportional to \(B^{2}.\)

    Example 3.8.6.

    A Tesla coil is constructed by wrapping \(N_{1}\) turns of wire along a length l of a hollow tube of cross-sectional area A and then wrapping a second coil \(N_{2}\) times around the first coil.
    1. What is the mutual inductance of the two coils?
    2. If \(N_{1} = 10,000\) turns, \(N_{2} = 100\) turns, \(l = 1 \,m,\) and the radius of the solenoid is 1 cm, what is the numerical value of the mutual inductance?
    3. If a radio frequency signal of \(1000 \,kHz\) is sent through the second coil such that the current oscillates with an amplitude of \(100 \,mA,\) what is the maximum average magnetic flux through each turn of the first coil?
    4. What is the maximum current through the first coil?
    5. What is the maximum induced emf in the first coil?
    6. What is the maximum induced back-emf in the first coil?
    Solution.
    Given: \(N_{1} = 10,000;\quad N_{2} =100;\quad l=1\,m;\) \(r=0.01\,m; \quad f=1.0\times10^{6} \,Hz;\quad I_{o} = 0.1\,A \)
    Equations:
    \begin{equation*} M = \frac{N_{2}\Phi_{B2}}{I_{1}};\quad \Phi_{B2} = \Phi_{B1} =B_{1}A \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} B_{1} = \mu_{o}\frac{N_{1}}{l}I_{1}; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} A = \pi r^{2} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \omega = 2\pi f; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} I_{2} = I_{o} \sin\omega t \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} M = \frac{N_{1}\Phi_{B1}}{I_{2}}; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} N_{1}I_{1} = N_{2}I_{2} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \varepsilon_{1} = -M\frac{\,dI_{2}}{\,dt}; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\varepsilon_{1}}{\varepsilon_{2}} = \frac{N_{1}}{N_{2}} \end{equation*}
    1. \begin{equation*} M = \frac{\mu_{o}A N_{1}N_{2}}{l}\Rightarrow A= 0.0003142 \,m^{2} \end{equation*}
    2. \begin{equation*} M= 3.948\times10^{-4} \,H; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \omega = 6.283\times10^{6} \,rev/s \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \Phi_{B1} = \frac{MI_{o}\sin\omega t}{N_{1}} \end{equation*}
    3. \begin{equation*} \Phi_{B1 max} = \frac{MI_{o}}{N_{1}} =3.948\times10^{-9} \,W_{b} \end{equation*}
    4. \begin{equation*} I_{1 max} = \frac{N_{2}I_{2 max}}{N_{1}} =1.0\times10^{-3}\, A \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \varepsilon_{1} = -M\omega I_{o}\cos\omega t \end{equation*}
    5. \begin{equation*} [\varepsilon_{1 max} = M\omega I_{o}= 248\, V \end{equation*}
    6. \begin{equation*} \varepsilon_{2 max} = \frac{N_{2}}{N_{1}}\varepsilon_{1 max} = 2.48\, V \end{equation*}

    Example 3.8.8.

    The figure below shows an R-L circuit. Assume \(\varepsilon = 12 \,V, \quad R = 10 \,\Omega,\) and \(L = 100 \,H.\) Find \(V_{ab}, \quad V_{bc},\quad V_{cd},\quad V_{ce},\) and \(I\text{,}\)
    1. with both switches open,
    2. immediately after switch \(S_{1}\) is closed,
    3. 1 hour after switch \(S_{1}\) is closed
    4. 4 s after switch \(S_{1}\) is closed.
    Solution.
    Given: \(\varepsilon = 12\,V, \, R=10\,\Omega, \,L= 100\,H \)
    Equations:
    \begin{equation*} V_{ab} =IR; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} V_{bc} = L\frac{dI}{dt}, \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \varepsilon -IR-L\frac{dI}{dt} = 0 \end{equation*}
    1. \(\displaystyle V_{ab} =0, \quad V_{bc} =0,\quad V_{cd}=12V,\quad V_{ce} = 0, \quad I=0\)
    2. \(\displaystyle V_{ab} =0, \quad V_{bc} =12\,V,\quad V_{cd}=0, \quad V_{ce} = -12\,V, \quad I=0\)
    3. \(\displaystyle [V_{ab} =12\,V,\quad V_{bc} =0,\quad V_{cd}=0, \quad V_{ce} = -12\,V,\quad I=1.2\,A\)
    4. Assume, \(I =I_{f}(1-e^{-t/\tau}),\) then
      \begin{equation*} \frac{dI}{dt} = \frac{I_{f}}{\tau}e^{-t/\tau}, \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} I_{f} =\frac{\varepsilon}{R} = 1.2\,A \end{equation*}
