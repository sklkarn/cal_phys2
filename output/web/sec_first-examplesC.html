    Section 1.8 Examples C

    Electric Potential.

    Example 1.8.1.

    An electric charge \(q_{1} = 10 \,\mu C\) remains located at the origin of a coordinate system. A second electrical charge \(q_{2} = 20 \,\mu C\) is moved from point to point.
    1. How much work is required by an external force to move the second charge along the x-axis from point (1 m, 0) to (2m, 0)?
    2. What is the electric potential at the point (1 m, 0) due to the charge at the origin?
    3. What is the potential difference between the points (1 m, 0) and (2 m, 0)?
    4. What is the potential difference between points (1 m, 0) and (0, 1 m)?
    5. How much work is required by an external force to move the second charge from point (1 m, 0) to (0, 1 m)?
    6. What is the integral \(\int\limits_{a}^{b}\vec{E}\cdot\vec{\,dl}\) along a circle from point (1 m, 0) to (0, 1 m)?
    Solution.
    Given: \(q_{1} = 1.00 \times 10^{-5} \,C; \) \(q_{2} = 2.00 \times 10^{-5} \,C;\) \(\epsilon_{o} = 8.85\times 10^{-12} \,C^{2}/N.m^{2}\)
    1. \begin{align*} \vec{F}_{elect} \amp =k\frac{q_{1}q_{2}}{r^{2}}\hat{r}\\ \vec{F}_{ext} \amp =-\vec{F}_{elect}; \end{align*}
      \(r =x;\quad \hat{r}=\hat{i};\quad \vec{\,dl}=\,dx(\hat{i})\)
      \begin{align*} W_{ab}\amp =\int\limits_{a}^{b}\vec{F}_{ext}\cdot \vec{\,dl} = -\int\limits_{1}^{2}F_{elect}\,dx \\ \amp =-k\int\limits_{1}^{2}\frac{q_{1}q_{2}}{x^{2}}\,dx =-k \left[-\frac{q_{1}q_{2}}{x}\right]_{1}^{2} \\ \amp = kq_{1}q_{2}\left(\frac{1}{2}-\frac{1}{1}\right) =-0.90 \,J \end{align*}
    2. \begin{equation*} V_{b}=k\frac{q_{1}}{r_{b}}=9.0\times 10^{4} \,V,\quad [r_{b}= 1\,m ] \end{equation*}
    3. \begin{align*} V_{c}\amp =k\frac{q_{1}}{r_{c}}=4.5\times 10^{4} \,V,\quad [r_{c}= 2\,m] \\ V_{bc}\amp =V_{b}-V_{c} = 4.5\times 10^{4} \,V \end{align*}
    4. \begin{align*} V_{d}\amp =k\frac{q_{1}}{r_{d}}=9.0\times 10^{4} \,V,\quad [r_{d}= 1\,m]\\ V_{bd}=V_{b}-V_{d} \amp =0 \end{align*}
    5. \begin{equation*} W_{bd}=q_{2}(V_{d}-V_{b}) = 0 \end{equation*}
    6. \begin{equation*} \int\limits_{b}^{d}\vec{E}\cdot \vec{dl}=-V_{bd} =0 \end{equation*}
      The same result is obtained by taking \(\vec{E}\) is always perpendicular to \(\vec{\,dl}\) along the circular path of integration, so
      \begin{align*} \vec{E}\cdot\vec{dl} \amp = 0 \\ \text{and} \quad \int\limits_{b}^{d}\vec{E}\cdot \vec{dl} \amp =0. \end{align*}

    Example 1.8.2.

    The electric field is given by the equation
    \begin{equation*} \vec{E} = c y2 \hat{i} + 2c xy \hat{j}, \end{equation*}
    where \(c = 1000 \,V/m^{3}.\)
    1. How much work does the electrical field do on a charge of \(-2 \,\mu C\) taken from point (0, 0) along the x-axis to (1 m, 0) and then parallel to the y-axis to (1 m, 2 m)?
    2. How much electrical work is done when the charge is moved along a straight line from (0, 0) to (1 m, 2 m)?
    3. What is the equation for the electric potential of this field assuming the origin has a potential of zero?
    4. What is the electric potential at point (1 m, 2 m)?
    Solution.
    Given: \(q=-2.00\times10^{-6} \,C; \quad c=1000 \,V/m^{3};\)
    \begin{equation*} \vec{E}=c y^2 \hat{i} + 2c xy \hat{j} \end{equation*}
    \begin{align*} W \amp = q \int\vec{E}\cdot\vec{\,dl} \\ \amp = q \left(\int E_{x}\,dx+\int E_{y}\,dy \right) \\ \amp = qc\left(\int y^{2}\,dx+\int 2xy \,dy \right) \end{align*}
    1. For path AB: \(y = 0, \quad\,dy = 0,\) and x goes from 0 to 1 m, so
      \begin{equation*} W_{AB} = 0. \end{equation*}
      For path BC: \(x = 1 m, \quad \,dx = 0,\) and y goes from 0 to 2 m, so
      \begin{equation*} W_{BC} = xqc\int\limits_{0}^{2} 2y \,dy = xqc \left[y^{2}\right]_{0}^{2} = 8.00\times 10^{-3}\,J \end{equation*}
      For total path
      \begin{equation*} W_{ABC} = W_{AB} + W_{BC} = 8.00 \times 10^{-3} \,J \end{equation*}
    2. For path AC: \(y = 2x,\quad \,dy = 2 \,dx,\) and x goes from 0 to 1 m, so
      \begin{align*} W_{AC} \amp = qc\left(\int\limits_{0}^{1} (2x)^{2} \,dx+\int\limits_{0}^{1} (2x)(2x)2\,dx \right) \\ \amp = qc \left[\frac{4x^{3}}{3}+\frac{8x^{3}}{3}\right]_{0}^{1}= 8.00\times 10^{-3} \,J \end{align*}
    3. \begin{align*} V \amp = -\int\vec{E}\cdot\vec{\,dl}\\ \amp =-\left(\int E_{x}\,dx+\int E_{y}\,dy\right) \\ \amp = -c\left(\int y^{2}\,dx+\int 2xy\,dy\right) \end{align*}
      This must be true along any path, so choose the path \(y = 0, \quad \,dy = 0,\) while x goes from 0 to x; then hold x constant with \(\,dx = 0\) while y goes from 0 to y. For the first part of this path, \(V = 0,\) and for the second part
      \begin{equation*} V(x,y) = -c\int\limits_{0}^{y} 2xy\,dy = -c x \left[y^{2}\right]_{0}^{y}= -c x y^2 \end{equation*}
    4. \begin{equation*} V(1 \,m, \, 2 \,m) = - 4000 \,V \end{equation*}

    Example 1.8.3.

    A charge \(q_{1} = -1 \,\mu C\) is located at (-1 m, 1 m). A second charge \(q_{2} = +2 \,\mu C\) is located at (2 m, -1 m). How much work is done by the electrical field of these two charges when a third charge \(q_{3} = +3 \,\mu C\) is moved from the origin to the point (1 m, 1 m)?
    Solution.
    Given: \(q_{1} = -1.00 \times 10^{-6} \,C;\quad q_{1} = 2.00 \times 10^{-6} \,C; \quad q_{3} = 3.00 \times 10^{-6} \,C. \)
    \begin{align*} V_{o} \amp = k\frac{q_{1}}{r_{o1}}+ k\frac{q_{2}}{r_{o2}};\\ r_{o1}\amp = \sqrt{1^{2}+1^{2}} \,m; \\ r_{o2} \amp = \sqrt{2^{2}+1^{2}} \,m; \\ V_{o}\amp = 1.685\times 10^{3} \,V\\ V_{p} \amp = k\frac{q_{1}}{r_{p1}} + k\frac{q_{2}}{r_{p2}};\\ r_{p1}\amp = 2 \,m;\\ r_{p2} \amp = \sqrt{1^{2}+2^{2}} \,m;\\ V_{p}\amp = 3.550\times 10^{3} \,V \\ W_{op}(field)\amp = -W_{op}(external) = q_{3} (V_{p} - V_{o});\\ W_{op}(field)\amp= 5.59\times10^{-3} \,J \end{align*}

    Example 1.8.4.

    The electric potential near the origin is given by
    \begin{equation*} V(r) = \frac{1}{2}k r^{2}, \end{equation*}
    where \(k = 10^{4} \,V/m^{2}\) and \(r\) is the magnitude of the position vector
    \begin{equation*} \vec{r} = x \hat{i}+ y \hat{j} + z\hat{ k}. \end{equation*}
    Find
    1. \(\displaystyle V(x,y,z),\)
    2. \(\displaystyle \vec{E}(x,y,z),\)
    3. \(\displaystyle \vec{E}( \vec{r} ),\)
    4. \(V \) at (0.3m, 0.4m, 0),
    5. \(E\) at (0.3m, 0.4m, 0),
    6. the speed of a particle of mass \(m = 1 \,g\) and charge \(q = 10 \,\mu C\) when passing through the origin after being released from rest at point (0.3 m, 0.4 m, 0). Note: Ignore gravity in this problem.
    Solution.
    1. \begin{align*} V(r)\amp =\frac{1}{2}k r^{2};\\ k\amp =10^{4}\,V/m^{2},\\ m\amp =10^{-3}\,kg,\\ q\amp=10^{-5}C \\ \therefore\quad V \amp =\frac{1}{2} k (x^{2}+y^{2}+z^{2}) \\ r^{2}\amp = x^{2}+y^{2}+z^{2}] \end{align*}
    2. \begin{equation*} \vec{E}=-\vec{\nabla} V =-kx \hat{i}-ky \hat{j}-kz \hat{k} \end{equation*}
    3. \(\displaystyle \vec{E}=-k\vec{r}\)
    4. \(\vec{E}=-\vec{\nabla} V =1250 \,V; \) [ V(0.3 m,0.4 m,0)]
    5. \(\displaystyle \vec{E}(0.3\,m,0.4\,m,0) = -3000\, N/C \hat{i}-4000 \,N/C \hat{j}\)
    6. \begin{equation*} U=qV; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} U=\frac{1}{2}mv^{2}; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} v=\sqrt{\frac{2qV}{m}}=5 \,m/s \end{equation*}

    Example 1.8.5.

    If a charged conducting sphere (A) of radius \(r_{1}\) and charge \(q_{1}\) is connected to an uncharged conducting sphere (B) of radius \(r_{2}\) with a very thin and long wire such that \(l \gt \gt (r_{1}+r_{2})\text{.}\) Find the charge on sphere (B). Where \(l\) is the distance between the conductors.
    Solution.
    Since sphere (B) is placed at very far away from sphere (A) the potential of sphere (B) must be zero initially. Potential at sphere (A) is
    \begin{equation*} V_{1}=\frac{kq_{1}}{r_{1}}. \end{equation*}
    Potential at sphere (B) is
    \begin{equation*} V_{2} = 0, \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} i.e., \quad V_{1} \gt V_{2} \end{equation*}
    Hence charge starts flowing from sphere (A) to sphere (B) till they acquire the same potential, i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} V'_{1}=V'_{2}. \end{equation*}
    Now, once they reaches at the same potential then charge left on sphere (A) is
    \begin{equation*} q'=q_{1}-q_{2}, \end{equation*}
    where \(q_{2}\) is the final charge transferred onto sphere (B). Therefore final potential of sphere (A) is
    \begin{equation*} V'_{1} = \frac{kq'}{r_{1}} \end{equation*}
    and final potential at sphere (B) is
    \begin{equation*} V'_{2} = \frac{kq_{2}}{r_{2}}. \end{equation*}
    But
    \begin{equation*} V'_{1} = V'_{2} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \frac{kq'}{r_{1}}=\frac{kq_{2}}{r_{2}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad q' r_{2} = q_{2}r_{1} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad (q_{1}-q_{2})r_{2} = q_{2}r_{1} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad q_{2} = \frac{q_{1}r_{2}}{r_{1}+r_{2}} \end{equation*}

    Example 1.8.6.

    The vertical deflection plates of a Cathode-Ray Tube (CRT) are each L = 10 cm square plates spaced d = 2 cm apart and located D = 20 cm in front of the fluorescent screen as shown below. An electron beam enters the region between plates and just misses the upper plate when it exits. The voltage between the plates is 100 V.
    1. What is the magnitude and direction of the electric field between the plates?
    2. What is the electric charge on the upper plate?
    3. What is the force on an electron when between plates?
    4. How much time does an electron spend between the plates?
    5. What is the speed of the electron when it enters the electric field?
    6. What was the potential difference in the electron gun that accelerated the electron up to this speed?
    7. What is the electron\rq s speed when it leaves the plates?
    8. How far is the electron deflected when it hits the screen?
    Solution.
    Given: \(L = 0.1 \,m;\quad d = 0.02 \,m; \quad D = 0.2 \,m;\) \(V_{o} = 100 \,V; \epsilon_{o} = 8.85\times10^{-12} \,C^{2}/N.m^{2};\)
    \begin{equation*} e = 1.60\times10^{-19} \,C; \quad m = 9.11\times10^{-31} \,kg \end{equation*}
    1. \begin{equation*} \vec{E}= -\vec{\nabla V} = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial r}; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad V_{o}=\vec{E} \cdot\vec{d}; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad E= -\frac{V_{o}}{d}y \end{equation*}

    Example 1.8.7.

    Find the electrical potential at a point due to a non-uniform linear charge density such that \(\lambda=a|y|\) of a rod of length \(2a\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    Consider a rod of length \(2a\) having non-linear charge density \(\lambda=a|y|\) so that
    \begin{equation*} \begin{cases} |y| = +y \quad \text{for +ve y-axis} \\ \quad = -y \quad \text{for -ve y-axis.} \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Potential at point P due to charge element \(\,dq\) is
    \begin{equation*} \,dV = \frac{k\,dq}{r}\quad \text{where}\quad r=\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}. \end{equation*}
    Therefore potential at P due to entire rod,
    \begin{equation*} V=\int\,dV=\int\limits_{-a}^{a}\frac{k\lambda\,dy}{\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}} \quad [\because\lambda=\frac{\,dq}{\,dy}] \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad V=\int\,dV=\int\limits_{-a}^{a}\frac{k a|y|\,dy}{\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = \int\limits_{-a}^{0}\frac{-k a y\,dy}{\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}} + \int\limits_{0}^{a}\frac{k a y\,dy}{\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad V = -k a \left[\int\limits_{-a}^{0}\frac{ y\,dy}{\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}} - \int\limits_{0}^{a}\frac{ y\,dy}{\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}}\right] \end{equation*}
    Lets solve
    \begin{equation*} \mathscr{I} = \frac{ y\,dy}{\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}} \end{equation*}
    From figure,
    \begin{equation*} x^{2}+y^{2}=r^{2} \end{equation*}
    so that \(2y\,dy=2r\,dr\)
    \begin{equation*} or,\quad y\,dy=r\,dr \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad I = \int \frac{r\,dr}{r}=\int \,dr=r=\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}} \end{equation*}
    Hence,
    \begin{equation*} V=-ka\left[\left(\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}\right)_{-a}^{0}-\left(\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}\right)_{0}^{a}\right] \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =-ka\left[\left(\sqrt{x^{2}}-\sqrt{x^{2}+a^{2}}\right)-\left(\sqrt{x^{2}+a^{2}}-\sqrt{x^{2}}\right)\right] \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =-ka\left[\left(|x|-\sqrt{x^{2}+a^{2}}\right)-\left(\sqrt{x^{2}+a^{2}}-|x|\right)\right] \end{equation*}
    since \(-a\) to \(0\) \(|x|\) is -ve hence first term is -ve, ie.,
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad V=2ka\left[|x|-\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}\right] \end{equation*}

    Example 1.8.8.

    A parallel-plate capacitor is made from two square sheets of metal each 2 mm thick and 20 cm on a side. The distance between the plates is 1 mm and the voltage between them is 1000 V. Find
    1. the capacitance of the capacitor,
    2. the charge on each plate,
    3. the charge density (charge per unit area),
    4. the electric field between the plates,
    5. the energy stored in the capacitor,
    6. the energy density of the electric field.
    Solution.
    Given: \(L = 0.2 \,m;\quad \epsilon_{o} = 8.85\times10^{-12} \,F/m; \) \(d = 1.0\times10^{-3} \,m;\quad V = 10^{3} \,V \)

      \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad A= L^{2} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} A=0.04 \,m^{2};\quad C=35.4 \,nF \end{equation*}
    2. \begin{equation*} Q = CV =0.354 \,\mu C \end{equation*}
    3. \begin{equation*} \sigma=\frac{Q}{A}= 8.85\times10^{-6} \,C/m^{2} \end{equation*}
    4. \begin{equation*} V = ED;\quad E = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_{o}} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} E= \frac{V}{d}= 1.0\times10^{6} \,V/m \end{equation*}
    5. \begin{equation*} U=\frac{1}{2}QV = 1.77\times 10^{-4} \,J \end{equation*}
    6. \begin{equation*} u=\frac{U}{Ad};\quad u=\frac{1}{2}\epsilon_{o}E^{2} = 4.425 \,J/m^{3} \end{equation*}

    Example 1.8.9.

    A spherical capacitor consists of two concentric conducting shells. A charge of \(-Q\) resides on the outer surface of the inner shell of radius \(r_{a}\) and a charge \(+Q\) on the inner surface of the outer shell of radius \(r_{b}\) as shown in the given figure.
    1. What is the charge per unit area on the inner shell?
    2. On the outer shell?
    3. What is the electric field between the shells?
    4. What is the potential difference between the shells?
    5. What is the capacitance of the spherical capacitor?
    6. Which of the previous the results would have been different if the charges had been reversed.
    Solution.
    1. \begin{equation*} \sigma_{a}=\frac{Q}{A_{a}}; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad A_{a}= \pi r^{2}_{a}; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad \sigma_{a}=-\frac{Q}{4\pi r^{2}_{a}} \end{equation*}
    2. \begin{equation*} \sigma_{b}=\frac{Q}{A_{b}}; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad A_{b}= \pi r^{2}_{b} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad \sigma_{b}=-\frac{Q}{4\pi r^{2}_{b}} \end{equation*}
    3. \begin{equation*} \text{spherical charge} \quad E=-k\frac{Q}{ r^{2}} \end{equation*}
    4. \begin{equation*} V_{a}=-k \frac{Q}{r_{a}}; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} V_{b}=-k \frac{Q}{r_{b}}; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} V=V_{ba} =V_{b}-V_{a} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} V= kQ\left[\frac{1}{r_{a}}-\frac{1}{r_{b}}\right] \end{equation*}
    5. \begin{equation*} C = \frac{Q}{V} = 4\pi\epsilon_{o}\frac{r_{a}r_{b}}{r_{b}-r_{a}} \end{equation*}
    6. Replace \(Q\) in the above equations with \(-Q.\) The sign on every answer would change except for the last. The capacitance would be the same.

    Example 1.8.12.

    A capacitor is made by sandwiching two thin sheets of aluminum between two layers of neoprene rubber and then rolled into a cylinder. Each of the sheets are 50 cm wide and 50 m long. The layers of rubber are 0.5 mm thick and have a dielectric constant of 6.7.
    1. What is the permittivity of neoprene?
    2. What is the capacitance of the capacitor?
    3. If 12 volts are applied across the capacitor, what is the electric field between the sheets?
    4. What is the charge density on the surface of the aluminum sheets?
    5. How much energy is stored in the capacitor?
    Solution.
    If the sheets were kept flat instead of being rolled up, we would have a flat-plate capacitor with only one side of each sheet charged (the one nearest the other sheet). But because of the rolling, the results is the same as if many sheets of alternating charge were stacked on top of one another. Therefore, electrical charge resides on both sides of each sheet (except for the outermost sheets which can be neglected). This doubles the effective area of the sheets making the area A of the capacitor twice the area \(A_{1}\) of one sheet. Otherwise, the rolled up capacitor is the same as a regular flat-plate capacitor.
    Given: \(W = 0.5 \,m; \quad L = 50 \,m; \) \(d=5.0\times10^{-4} \,m;\quad K =6.7:\quad V=12\,V; \) \(\epsilon_{o}=8.85\times10^{-12} \,F/m \)

    4. \begin{equation*} E = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon};\quad \sigma = \epsilon E = 1.42 \times 10^{-6} \,C/m^{2} \end{equation*}
    5. \begin{equation*} Q =CV; \quad U = \frac{1}{2} QV; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \quad Q = 7.12 \times 10^{-5} \,C; \quad U=4.27 \times 10^{-4} \,J \end{equation*}
