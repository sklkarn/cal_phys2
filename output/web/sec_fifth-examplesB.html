    Section 5.5 Examples B

    Example 5.5.1.

    A small fish of length 1 cm is located at the center of a thin spherical fishbowl 20 cm in diameter. The index of refraction of water is 1.33 and that of the glass fishbowl is 1.5.
    1. Does the thin layer of glass affect the refraction of the light rays?
    2. When viewed from outside, where does the fish appear to be located?
    3. What is its magnification?
    4. How long does the fish appear to be?
    Solution.
    Given: \(n_{1} =1,\, n_{2} = 1.5,\, \) and \(n_{3} = 1.33. \)
    1. \begin{equation*} n_{1}\sin\theta_{1}=n_{2}\sin\theta_{2}\quad \text{air to glass} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} n_{2}\sin\theta_{2}=n_{3}\sin\theta_{3}\quad \text{glass to water} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} n_{1}\sin\theta_{1}=n_{3}\sin\theta_{3}\quad \text{air to water} \end{equation*}
      Since the air to glass to water result for \(\theta_{3}\) is the same as that for air to water, the glass has no effect on the refraction.
    2. \begin{equation*} \frac{n}{s}+ \frac{n'}{s'}= \frac{n' -n}{R} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \text{or,} \quad \frac{1.33}{10}+ \frac{1}{s'}= \frac{1-1.33}{-10} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \Rightarrow s' = -10 \,cm \quad \text{(virtual at center) } \end{equation*}
    3. \begin{equation*} m=-\frac{ns'}{n's} =-\frac{(1.33) (-10)}{(1)(10)}=1.33 \quad \text{(enlarged)} \end{equation*}
    4. \begin{equation*} m=\frac{y'}{y} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \text{or,} \quad 1.33=\frac{y'}{1} \quad \Rightarrow \quad y' =1.33 \,cm \end{equation*}

    Example 5.5.2.

    A mosquito is found embedded in an amber sphere of radius of curvature 3.0 mm and of refractive index 1.6. The mosquito is located on the principal axis and appears to be embedded at 5.0 mm into the amber. How deep is the mosquito really? What is the magnification?
    Solution.
    Given: \(n_{a}=1.6, \quad n_{b}=1\) , \(R=-3 \,mm, \quad v=-5\,mm \quad (virtual image).\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{n_{a}}{s}+\frac{n_{b}}{s'} =\frac{n_{b}-n_{a}}{R} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1.6}{s}+\frac{1}{-5} =\frac{1-1.6}{-3} =0.2 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad s = 4.0 \,mm\quad \text{(real object)} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} m=-\frac{n_{a}s'}{n_{b}s} = \frac{1.6\times 5}{1\times 4}=2 \end{equation*}

    Example 5.5.3.

    A clear crystal ball of diameter 20 cm is made from solid glass of index of refraction 1.5. One half of the ball is silvered so as to form a mirror on the ball‚Äôs surface. A person whose face is 25 cm high and 30 cm from the ball looks at his reflection through the ball. Light travels from his face through the clear surface of the ball, is reflected from the silvered surface, and passes back through the clear surface so as to form a final image.
    1. Where is this final image located?
    2. What is the magnification of the complete optical system?
    3. What is the height of the final image?
    Solution.
    1. \begin{equation*} \frac{n_{1}}{s_{1}}+\frac{n'_{1}}{s'_{1}} = \frac{n'_{1}-n_{1}}{R_{1}} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \frac{1}{30}+\frac{1.5}{s'_{1}} = \frac{1.5-1}{10} \Longrightarrow s'_{1} = 90 \,cm \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} s'_{1} +s_{2} = D \Longrightarrow 90 +s_{2} =20 \Longrightarrow s_{2} = -70\,cm \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{s_{2}}+\frac{1}{s'_{2}}=\frac{2}{R_{2}} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \frac{1}{-70}+\frac{1}{s'_{2}}=\frac{2}{10} \Rightarrow s'_{2} =4.67 \,cm \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} s'_{2} +s_{3} = D \Longrightarrow 4.67 +s_{3} =20 \Longrightarrow s_{3} = 15.33 \,cm \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \frac{n_{3}}{s_{3}}+\frac{n'_{3}}{s'_{3}} = \frac{n'_{3}-n_{3}}{R_{3}} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \frac{1.5}{15.33}+\frac{1}{s'_{3}} = \frac{1-1.5}{-10} \Longrightarrow s'_{3} = -20.9 \,cm \end{equation*}
    2. \begin{equation*} m=-\frac{s'_{1}}{s_{1}}\times-\frac{s'_{2}}{s_{2}}\times-\frac{s'_{3}}{s_{3}} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} =\frac{-90}{30}\times\frac{-4.67}{-70}\times\frac{-(-20.9)}{15.33} \Rightarrow m = -0.273 \end{equation*}
    3. \begin{equation*} m=\frac{y'_{3}}{y1} \Rightarrow -0.273 = \frac{y'_{3}}{25}\Rightarrow y'_{3}= -6.82 \end{equation*}

    Example 5.5.4.

    A convex surface of radius of curvature 40 cm separates two media of refractive indices 4/3 and 3/2. An object is kept in the first medium at a distance of 20 cm from the surface. Calculate the position of the image?
    Solution.
    Given: \(n=4/3, \, n'=1.5,\, R= + 40 cm.\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{n}{s}+\frac{n'}{s'}=\frac{n'-n}{R} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or,\quad \frac{4}{3\times 20}+\frac{1.5}{s'}=\frac{1.5-4/3}{40} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \Rightarrow s'=-24 \,cm. \end{equation*}
    Which is virtual in nature and formed in the first medium just before the real object.

    Example 5.5.5.

    A small filament is at the center of a hollow glass sphere of inner and outer radii 8 cm and 9 cm, respectively. Calculate the image position of the filament when viewed from outside of the sphere. Assume refractive index of glass = 1.5.
    Solution.
    Given: \(n=1, \quad n'=1.5,\quad R_{1} = -8\,cm,\quad R_{2} = -9\,cm.\) For first concave surface,
    \begin{equation*} \frac{n}{s}+\frac{n'}{s'}=\frac{n'-n}{R_{1}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or,\quad \frac{1}{8}+\frac{1.5}{s'}=\frac{1.5-1}{-8} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \Rightarrow s'=-8 \,cm. \end{equation*}
    The image of the object is formed at the center of first sphere and is virtual.
    For second concave surface,
    \begin{equation*} \frac{n}{s}+\frac{n'}{s'}=\frac{n'-n}{R_{2}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or,\quad \frac{1}{9}+\frac{1.5}{s'}=\frac{1.5-1}{-9} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \Rightarrow s'=-9 \,cm. \end{equation*}
    Thus the final image is also formed at the center and is virtual.

    Example 5.5.6.

    Two fish are at the bottom of 10 m deep water. A wall between them blocks their view so that they cannot see each other directly. However, they know the presence each other through their mirror images created above the water surface. At least how far are they separated? Assume that the index of reflection of air and water is 1.00 and 1.52, respectively.
    Solution.
    For fish to see each other angle of incidence of the light from them to the water-air interface must suffers total internal reflection and must be greater than or equal to critical angle. Suppose the fish are at a distance r from each other which is a minimum distance for them to see each other then angle of incidence \(\angle i = \angle c\text{.}\) From Snell‚Äôs Law
    \begin{equation*} \eta_{1}\sin\theta_{1} =\eta_{2}\sin\theta_{2} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,} \quad 1.52 \sin c =1\sin 90 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \Rightarrow \quad c = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{1.52}\right)=41.14^{o} \end{equation*}
    Now from right angle \(\triangle OAP,\)
    \begin{equation*} \tan c = \frac{r/2}{d}=\frac{r}{2d} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} r=2d\tan c = 2\times 10\times \tan 41.14^{o}=17.47 \,m \end{equation*}

    Example 5.5.7.

    A ray of light is incident at an angle of \(60^{o}\) onto the surface of a pool of water. Part of the light is reflected and part refracted. What is the angle between the reflected ray and the refracted ray? Assume \(n(water) = 1.33.\)
    Solution.
    Given: \(\theta_{1}=60^{o},\quad n_{1}=1,\quad n_{3}=1.33\)
    \begin{equation*} \theta_{1} =\theta_{2}=60^{o} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} n_{1} \sin \theta_{1} = n_{3} \sin \theta_{3} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \theta_{3}=40.63^{o} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \theta =(90^{o}-\theta_{2})+(90^{o}-\theta_{3})\quad \Rightarrow \quad \theta=79.4^{o} \end{equation*}

    Example 5.5.8.

    A \(30-60-90^{o}\) prism of index of refraction 1.50 is oriented as shown below with its long face (Face 2) horizontally facing upward, a drop of oil of unknown index of refraction is placed on the top face of the prism, and a ray of light enters normally into the prism through its short face (Face 1) so as to strike the top face from below.
    1. What is the minimum index of refraction of the oil that will cause the internal ray to be totally reflected from the top face?
    2. What is the angle of incident that the internally reflected ray makes with the other face (Face 3) of the prism.
    3. Is the light totally reflected off the third face or is it refracted through the surface?
    4. If refracted, what is the angle of refraction as it emerges from the prism?
    Solution.
    1. \begin{equation*} n_{1} \sin \theta_{1} = n \sin \theta_{exit} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} or,\quad 1.5 \sin 60^{o} = n \sin 90^{o} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \Rightarrow n = 1.3 \end{equation*}
    2. \begin{equation*} \theta_{1} = \theta_{2} = 60^{o} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \text{but}\quad \theta_{1} =\theta+\theta_{3} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} or,\quad 60^{o} = 30^{o}+\theta_{3} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad \theta_{3} = 30^{o} \end{equation*}
    3. \(\because \) \quad \(\theta_{3} \lt \theta_{crit}\) then the ray will be refracted.
    4. \begin{equation*} $n_{3} \sin \theta_{3} = n_{4} \sin \theta_{5} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} or,\quad 1.5 \sin 30^{o} = 1 \sin \theta_{5} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \Rightarrow \theta_{5} = 48.6^{o} \end{equation*}

    Example 5.5.9.

    Light of wavelength 500 nm is incident normally on a glass plate coated with a thin coating of anti-reflecting material. If the glass has an index of refraction of 1.66 and the coating has an index of refraction of 1.58, find
    1. the wavelength of the light in the glass,
    2. in the coating.
    3. What is the minimum thickness of the coating that will produce destructive interference of the reflected light rays from the two surfaces of the coating?
    Solution.
    Given: \(\lambda_{o} =5.00\times10^{-7}\, m; \quad n_{g} = 1.66 \quad n_{c} = 1.58 \)
    1. \begin{equation*} \lambda_{g}=\frac{\lambda_{o}}{n_{g}}\Rightarrow \lambda_{g} = 3.01\times10^{-7} \,m \end{equation*}
    2. \begin{equation*} \lambda_{c}=\frac{\lambda_{o}}{n_{c}}\Rightarrow \lambda_{c} = 3.16\times10^{-7} \,m \end{equation*}
    3. \begin{equation*} 2d =\frac{\lambda_{c}}{2}\Rightarrow d = 7.91\times10^{-8} \,m. \end{equation*}

    Example 5.5.10.

    A thin double concave lens has a radius of curvature of 10 cm on both sides and is made of glass of index of refraction 1.5. A light bulb 2 cm high is located 50 cm in front of the lens along its principle axis.
    1. What is the focal length of the lens? Is it converging or diverging?
    2. Where is the image of the light bulb located? Is the image real or virtual?
    3. What is the height of the image? Is the image enlarged or reduced? Erect or inverted?
    Solution.
    1. \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{f} = (n-1)\left(\frac{1}{R_{1}}-\frac{1}{R_{2}}\right) \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} or,\quad \frac{1}{f} = (1.5-1)\left(\frac{1}{-10}-\frac{1}{10}\right) \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \Rightarrow f=-10\,cm \quad\text{diverging.} \end{equation*}
    2. \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{s}+\frac{1}{s'}=\frac{1}{f} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} or,\quad \frac{1}{50}+ \frac{n'}{s'}= \frac{1}{-10} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \Rightarrow s' = -12.5 \,cm \quad \text{virtual.} \end{equation*}
    3. \begin{equation*} m=\frac{y'}{y}=-\frac{s'}{s} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} or,\quad \frac{y'}{2} =-\frac{-12.5}{50} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \Rightarrow y' =0.5\, cm; \quad \text{reduced erect.} \end{equation*}

    Example 5.5.11.

    Calculate the focal length of a double convex lens of radius of curvature of each surface is 30 cm and refractive index of glass is 1.5.
    Solution.
    Given: \(n=1.5, \quad R_{1}=30 \,cm, \quad R_{2}=-30 \,cm.\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{f}=(n-1) \left(\frac{1}{R_{1}}-\frac{1}{R_{2}} \right) = (1.5-1) \left(\frac{1}{30}+\frac{1}{30} \right) = \frac{1}{30} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad f=30\, cm \end{equation*}

    Example 5.5.12.

    Calculate the focal length of a plano convex lens, the radius of curvature of convex surface is 40 cm and refractive index of glass is 1.5
    Solution.
    Given: \(n=1.5, \quad R_{1}=\infty,\quad R_{2}=-20 \,cm.\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{f}=(n-1) \left(\frac{1}{R_{1}}-\frac{1}{R_{2}} \right) = (1.5-1) \left(\frac{1}{\infty}+\frac{1}{40} \right) = \frac{1}{80} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad f=80\, cm \end{equation*}

    Example 5.5.13.

    A convex lens of focal length 24 cm and refractive index of glass is 1.5. Find the focal length of this lens if it is totally submerged into water of refractive index 1.33.
    Solution.
    Given: \(^{a}n_{g}=1.5,\quad ^{a}n_{w}=1.33, \quad f_{a}=24 \,cm, \quad f_{b}=?\)
    In air,
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{f_{a}}=(^{a}n_{g}-1) \left(\frac{1}{R_{1}}-\frac{1}{R_{2}} \right) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation} or, \quad \left(\frac{1}{R_{1}}-\frac{1}{R_{2}} \right) = \frac{1}{f_{a}(^{a}n_{g}-1)} =\frac{1}{24(1.5-1)} = \frac{1}{12} \tag{5.5.1} \end{equation}
    In water,
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{f_{b}}=(^{w}n_{g}-1) \left(\frac{1}{R_{1}}-\frac{1}{R_{2}} \right) \end{equation*}
    From the principle of reversibility of light,
    \begin{equation*} ^{w}n_{g}=^{w}n_{a}\times ^{a}n_{g} =\frac{^{a}n_{g}}{^{a}n_{w}} =\frac{1.5}{1.33} =1.125 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore \quad \frac{1}{f_{b}}=(1.125-1) \left( \frac{1}{12}\right) = \frac{12}{0.125} \end{equation*}
    using eqn. (5.5.1)
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad f_{b} = 96\, cm \end{equation*}

    Example 5.5.14.

    A concavo-convex lens has refractive index of 1.5 and radii of curvature of its surfaces are 10cm and 20 cm. The concave surface is upwards and is filled with oil of refractive index 1.6. Calculate the focal length of the oil-glass combination.
    Solution.
    Given: \(n_{g}=1.5,\quad n_{o}=1.6.\)
    Remember higher curvature means smaller radius of curvature. For the glass lens,
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{f_{1}}=(^{a}n_{g}-1) \left(\frac{1}{R_{1}}-\frac{1}{R_{2}} \right) = (1.5-1) \left(\frac{1}{10}-\frac{1}{20}\right) = \frac{1}{40} \end{equation*}
    For the oil lens,
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{f_{2}}=(^{a}n_{o}-1) \left(\frac{1}{R_{1}}-\frac{1}{R_{2}} \right) = (1.6-1) \left(\frac{1}{\infty}+\frac{1}{20}\right) = \frac{3}{100} \end{equation*}
    Therefore the equivalent focal length of the combination is given by,
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{F}=\frac{1}{f_{1}}+\frac{1}{f_{2}}= \frac{1}{40}+\frac{3}{100} = \frac{11}{200} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad F = 18.18 \,cm \end{equation*}

    Example 5.5.15.

    The objective glass lens of a telescope is an achromat of focal length 90 cm. If the dispersive powers of the materials of the lenses are 0.024 and 0.036, calculate their focal lengths.
    Solution.
    Given: \(\omega_{1} =0.024,\quad \omega_{2}=0.036,\quad F = 90 \,cm.\)
    At achromatic condition,
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\omega_{1}}{f_{1}} + \frac{\omega_{2}}{f_{2}} =0 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or,\quad \frac{1}{f_{2}} = -\frac{\omega_{1}}{f_{1}\omega_{2}} = - \frac{2}{3f_{1}} \end{equation*}
    Also,
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{F}=\frac{1}{f_{1}}+\frac{1}{f_{2}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or,\quad \frac{1}{90}=\frac{1}{f_{1}}-\frac{2}{3f_{1}} =\frac{1}{3f_{1}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad f_{1}=\frac{90}{3}=30 cm;\quad f_{2}= -\frac{3f_{1}}{2} =-45\,cm \end{equation*}

    Example 5.5.16.

    Find the dispersive power of crown and flint glass from the following data.
    \(n_{r}\) \(n\) \(n_{b}\)
    Crown \(1.5145\) \(1.5170 \) \(1.5230 \)
    Flint \(1.6444\) \(1.652\) \(1.6637\)
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} \omega=\frac{n_{b}-n_{r}}{n-1} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad \omega_{c} = \frac{1.523-1.5145}{1.517-1} = 0.0164; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{and}\quad \omega_{f} = \frac{1.6637-1.6444}{1.652-1} = 0.0296. \end{equation*}

    Example 5.5.17.

    In vacuum, an electromagnetic wave has speed \(c= 3 \times10^{8} \,m/s\) and frequency \(f = 6.0 \times 10^{14} \,Hz.\) It enters a material whose index of refraction is n = 1.40.
    1. Find the speed of the wave in the material.
    2. Find the frequency of the wave in the material.
    3. Find the wavelength of the wave in the material.
    4. Human can see electromagnetic wave in the range of wavelength from 390 nm to 750 nm in vacuum. Is the electromagnetic wave of frequency \(f = 8.50 \times 10^{14} \,Hz\) in vacuum visible light, infrared, or ultraviolet?
    Solution.
    1. \begin{equation*} n=\frac{c}{v} \Rightarrow v=\frac{c}{n} =\frac{3 \times10^{8} \,m/s}{1.4}= 2.14\times 10^{8} \,m/s \end{equation*}
    2. Frequency does not change as the wave goes from one medium to another. Therefore \(f_{a}=f_{b}=6.0 \times 10^{14} \,Hz.\)
    3. \begin{equation*} v=f\lambda \Rightarrow \lambda = \frac{v}{f}= \frac{2.14\times 10^{8} \,m/s}{6.0 \times 10^{14}\, Hz} = 356 \,nm \end{equation*}
    4. \begin{equation*} \lambda = \frac{c}{f} = \frac{3 \times10^{8} \,m/s}{8.50 \times 10^{14} \,Hz} = 352 \,nm \quad (UV light.) \end{equation*}
