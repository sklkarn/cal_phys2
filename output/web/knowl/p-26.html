The force of attraction or repulsion between any two point charges is directly proportional to the product of these charges and inversely proportional to the square of distance between them. The direction of force is always acting along the line joining these two point charges. If \(F_{12}\) is a force acting on charge \(q_{1}\) due to charge \(q_{2},\) \(F_{21}\) is a force acting on charge \(q_{2}\) due to charge \(q_{1},\) and \(r_{12} \) is a distance between the two charges (Figure 1.1.4) then from Coulumb’s law
\begin{equation} \vec{F} = k\frac{q_{1}q_{2}}{r^{2}}\hat{r} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_{o}}\frac{q_{1}q_{2}}{r^{2}}\hat{r}\tag{1.1.1} \end{equation}
where \(\hat{r}\) is unit vector along line joining the two charges, Coulomb’s constant of free space, \(k = 9.00 \times 10^{9} \,N m^{2}/C^{2}\text{;}\) and permittivity of free space, \(\epsilon_{o} = 8.85 \times 10^{-12}\,C^{2}/N m^{2}.\) Permittivity is an ability of a medium to store electrical energy in an electric field. In other words, permittivity is the extent to which a medium permits electrical lines of force between the charges. It is a resistance of a medium in forming electric fields. It describes the effect of a material in an electrical field. The higher the permittivity, the more the material tends to reduce electrical field (due to polarization) that set up it.
