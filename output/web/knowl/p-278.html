Current, \(I\text{:}\) An electric current is defined as the transfer of charge through any area of cross-section per unit time. If a net charge \(q\) passes through any cross-sectional area of a conductor in time \(t,\) then electric current is given by
\begin{equation} I=\frac{q}{t}.\tag{2.0.1} \end{equation}
It’s unit is Ampere, i.e. one ampere of current represents one coulomb of charge passes through a conductor in one second. It is a scalar quantity but it’s conventional direction of motion is taken as the direction of flow of positive charges. In a conductor current flows solely due to the motion of electrons, hence the direction of current is opposite to electron-flow. From the law of conservation of charges current remains constant in a conductor irrespective of the local area of cross-section. When the direction of motion of charge does not change with time, the current is said to be a direct current, or DC, but when the direction of charge motion reverses periodically, the current is known as alternating current, or AC.
