When lower arm of the loop enters into external magnetic field flux starts increasing in the loop and hence loop induces its current in such a way that it could oppose that change. Consequently, loop induced magnetic field, \(\vec{B}_{ind} \) just opposite to external magnetic field, \(\vec{B} \text{.}\) Meaning if external field \(\vec{B}\) is pointed into the page then induced field \(\vec{B}_{ind}\) by the loop is pointed out of the page. Now from Right Hand Thumb rule the induced current, I in the loop must be counterclockwise. As current, I is flowing in the conductor of width, w inside the external field \(\vec{B}\text{,}\) it starts experiencing a magetic force \(\vec{F}_{b}\) upwards which can be found by Flemingâ€™s Left Hand Rule: Forefinger along \(\vec{B} \text{,}\) middlefinger along currenet I, then thumb gives the direction of force \(\vec{F}_{b} \) upward. Now, emf induced in the loop is given by
\begin{equation*} e = -\frac{\,d\phi}{\,dt} = -B l \frac{\,dy}{\,dt} = -\,B \,l\, v \end{equation*}
As B and w are constant and loop is falling into the field along width w where we have considered \(\,dy\) as small elementry width of the loop. Since R is the resistance of the loop, we have magnitude of emf, \(e = IR.\)
\begin{equation} \therefore\quad IR = Blv \tag{3.8.1} \end{equation}
\begin{equation} also, \quad F_{b} = BIl \tag{3.8.2} \end{equation}
Using eqns. (3.8.1) and (3.8.2), we have-
\begin{equation} F_{b} = \frac{B^{2}l^{2}v }{R} \tag{3.8.3} \end{equation}
Note that the magnetic force on the left and right arms of the loop cancell out each other and there is no magnetic force acting on the top arm. Now before sufficient portion of the loop falls into the magnetic field the magnetidue of \(F_{b}\) keeps increasing and finally gets balanced by the \(F_{g}. \) At this point \(v\) becomes constant called terminal velocity, \(v_{t}.\) Hence,
\begin{equation*} F_{b} = F_{g} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} or, \quad \frac{B^{2}l^{2}v_{t} }{R} = mg \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \therefore\quad v_{t} = \frac{mgR}{B^{2}l^{2}} \end{equation*}
Here, \(v_{t}\) proportional to R and inversely proportional to \(B^{2}.\)
