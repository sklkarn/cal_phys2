  1. If the electric field at the surface of a conductor has a component parallel to the surface, the charges on the surface will move in that direction. Since they do not move in the electrostatic situation, the electric field must be everywhere perpendicular to the surface of the conductor.
  2. Pick a Gaussian surface surrounding a small enough region of the conductor’s surface so the charge density is constant. Let \(\,dq\) be the charge enclosed by the Gaussian surface and \(\,dA\) the area of the surface of the conductor enclosed by the Gaussian surface. Then the charge per unit area of the conductor in that region of space is \(\sigma = \,dq/\,dA.\)
  3. Because \(\sigma\) is constant in this small region of space, due to symmetry the electric field must be perpendicular to the surface of the conductor and constant at a distance d from the surface. Consider a cylindrical Gaussian surface as shown in given figures. Surfaces \(S_{1}\) and \(S_{2}\) are parallel to the conducting plane and surface \(S_{3}\) is cylindrical surface perpendicular to the conductor’s surface and parallel to electric field as shown in figure below.
    For a conductor electric field is only out side of the conductor but within it electric field is zero as shown in figure at right. In charged conductors the excess charges have distributed themselves optimally further away from one another so as to reduce the total amount of repulsive forces. Once a charged conductor has reached the state of electrostatic equilibrium, there is no further motion of charge about the surface as shown in figure below.
  4. The following equations then apply:
    \begin{equation*} \Phi_{S_{1}} = E dA; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \Phi_{S_{2}} = 0; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \Phi_{S_{3}} = 0; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \Phi =\Phi_{S_{1}}+\Phi_{S_{2}}+\Phi_{S_{3}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \Phi = \frac{dq}{\epsilon_{o}}; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \sigma = \,dq/\,dA \end{equation*}
  5. Solving these equations simultaneously for E gives:
    \begin{equation*} E=\frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_{o}} \end{equation*}
