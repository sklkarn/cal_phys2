We know that if a conductor of length \(L\) has a potential difference across its ends is \(V\) then electric field can be given as [(1.7.1)]
\begin{equation*} E= \frac{V}{L} \end{equation*}
and if current, \(I\) is flowing in this conductor of area of cross-section \(A\text{,}\) then current density is \(J=\frac{I}{A} \text{.}\) Now using Ohm’s law, we have
\begin{equation*} E=\frac{IR}{L} = \left(\frac{RA}{L}\right)J \end{equation*}
In this expression resistance \(R\) of the conductor of the given length \(L\) and area of cross-section \(A\) are all constant. Hence we can write electrical field as
\begin{equation*} E=\rho J \end{equation*}
where
\begin{equation} \rho= R\frac{A}{L}\tag{2.1.4} \end{equation}
is known as a specific resistance or resistivity of the conductor. It is a property of the material. Different material has a different resistivity. If a conductor of length \(L=1\,m\) has a cross-section area \(A=1\,m^{2}\) then
\begin{equation*} \rho=R \end{equation*}
i.e., resistivity of a conductor is a resistance of a unit length of the conductor of unit area cross-section. It’s unit is \(\Omega \,m \)
\begin{equation*} R = \frac{\rho L}{A} \end{equation*}
The reciprocal of resistivty is known as conductivity,
\begin{equation*} \sigma=\frac{1}{\rho} \end{equation*}
Such expression allows us to compare electrical field with current density such as
\begin{equation*} E=\rho J \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or}\quad J=\sigma E \end{equation*}
