Consider a symmetrical cylindrical Gaussian surface around the charge on an infinitely charged sheet of surface charge density is \(\sigma \) as shown in Figure 1.4.7. Now electric flux, \(\phi \) through the surfaces \(S_{1}, \quad S_{2},\) and \(S_{3}\) is given by
\begin{equation*} \phi=\phi_{1}+\phi_{2}+\phi_{3} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \phi=\int \vec{E}_{1}\cdot\vec{\,ds}_{1}+\int \vec{E}_{2}\cdot\vec{\,ds}_{2}+\int \vec{E}_{3}\cdot\vec{\,ds}_{3} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \phi = E_{1}A +E_{2}A + 0 \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \phi = (E_{1} +E_{2})A \end{equation*}
But from Gauss’s law,
\begin{equation*} \phi=\frac{\,dq}{\epsilon_{o}} = \frac{\sigma A}{\epsilon_{o}} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \therefore \quad (E_{1} +E_{2})A = \frac{\sigma A}{\epsilon_{o}} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad (E_{1} +E_{2})=\frac{\sigma }{\epsilon_{o}} \end{equation*}
Now because of surface symmetry around the charge
\begin{equation*} E_{1}=E_{2} =E \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \therefore \quad E = \frac{\sigma}{2\epsilon_{o}} \end{equation*}
That is, field is independent upon the distance of the point as long as the point is not significantly far away. Electric field is normal to the charged sheet except at the edges where it is emitting radially out ward in all possible directions. Hence, Gaussian law can not be applied to determined field at a point for a finite charged conductor.
