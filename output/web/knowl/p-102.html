Consider a symmterical cylindrical Gaussian surface around the charge on an infinitly charged wire of linear charge density is \(\lambda\) as shown in Figure 1.4.5.(a). Now electric flux, \(\phi \) through the surfaces \(S_{1}, \quad S_{2},\) and \(S_{3}\) is given by
\begin{equation*} \phi=\phi_{1}+\phi_{2}+\phi_{3} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \phi=\iint \vec{E}\cdot\vec{\,ds}_{1}+\iint \vec{E}\cdot\vec{\,ds}_{2}+\iint \vec{E}\cdot\vec{\,ds}_{3} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \phi= 0+0+\iint \vec{E}\cdot\vec{\,ds}_{3} = \iint E \,ds = E(2\pi rl) \end{equation*}
From Gauss’s law
\begin{equation*} \phi = \frac{\,dq}{\epsilon_{o}} = \frac{\lambda l}{\epsilon_{o}} \end{equation*}
\(\,dq \) is the charge enclosed in the Gaussian surface. Hence,
\begin{equation*} \phi= \frac{\lambda l}{\epsilon_{o}}=E(2\pi rl) \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \therefore \quad E=\frac{\lambda}{2\pi\epsilon_{o}r} \end{equation*}
ie., Electric field is inversely proportional to distance of the point from a charged rod [Figure 1.4.5.(b)].
