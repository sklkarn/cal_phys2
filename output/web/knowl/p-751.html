  1. Open Pipe: An organ pipe which is open at its both ends is an open organ pipe. Flute, Trumpet, etc. are open organ pipes. Consider an open pipe of length, L whose both ends are open. When vibration is set at one end it reflects from the other end and produces standing waves of fundamental frequency \(f_{o}\) as shown in Figure 4.5.2.
    \begin{equation*} \text{Now}\quad L=\frac{\lambda_{1}}{4}+\frac{\lambda_{1}}{4}= \frac{\lambda_{1}}{2} \qquad \Rightarrow \lambda_{1} = 2L \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad f_{1} =\frac{v}{\lambda_{1}}= \frac{v}{2L}=f_{o} \quad \text{I Harmonics or fundamental frequency.} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{again}\quad L=\frac{\lambda_{2}}{4} +\frac{\lambda_{2}}{2}+\frac{\lambda_{2}}{4} = \lambda_{2} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \Rightarrow \lambda_{2} = L \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{and} \quad f_{2} = \frac{v}{\lambda_{2}} =\frac{v}{L} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad f_{2} = \frac{v}{L}=2\left(\frac{v}{2L}\right)=2f_{o} \quad \text{II Harmonics or I overtone.} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{similarly}\quad L=\frac{\lambda_{3}}{2} +\frac{\lambda_{3}}{2}+\frac{\lambda_{3}}{2} = 3\frac{\lambda_{3}}{2} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \Rightarrow \lambda_{3} = \frac{2L}{3} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{and} \quad f_{3} = \frac{v}{\lambda_{3}} =3\left(\frac{v}{2L}\right)=3f_{o} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad f_{3} = 3f_{o} \quad \text{III Harmonics or II overtone.} \end{equation*}
    Hence the normal modes of vibration of an open pipe is given by
    \begin{equation*} f_{n}=nf_{o}\qquad \text{where}\quad n=1,2,3,\cdots. \end{equation*}
  2. Closed Pipe: An organ pipe which is open at its one ends and closed at another is a closed organ pipe. Clarinet, whistle, bottle, etc. are closed organ pipes. Consider a closed pipe of length, L whose one end is open and another is closed. When vibration is set at open end it reflects from the other end and produces standing waves of fundamental frequency \(f_{o}\) as shown in Figure 4.5.3.
    \begin{equation*} \text{Now}\quad L=\frac{\lambda_{1}}{4} \qquad \Rightarrow \lambda_{1} = 4L \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad f_{1} =\frac{v}{\lambda_{1}}= \frac{v}{4L}=f_{o} \quad [\text{I Harmonics or fundamental frequency.}] \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{again}\quad L=\frac{\lambda_{2}}{2} +\frac{\lambda_{2}}{4} = 3\left(\frac{\lambda_{2}}{4}\right) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \Rightarrow \lambda_{2} = \frac{4L}{3} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{and} \quad f_{2} = \frac{v}{\lambda_{2}} =3\left(\frac{v}{4L}\right)=3f_{o} \quad [\text{III Harmonics or I overtone.}] \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{similarly}\quad L=\frac{\lambda_{3}}{4} +\frac{\lambda_{3}}{2}+\frac{\lambda_{3}}{2} = 5\left(\frac{\lambda_{3}}{4}\right) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \Rightarrow \lambda_{3} = \frac{4L}{5} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{and} \quad f_{3} = \frac{v}{\lambda_{3}} =5\left(\frac{v}{4L}\right)=5f_{o} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad f_{3} = 5f_{o} \quad [\text{V Harmonics or II overtone.}] \end{equation*}
    Hence the normal modes of vibration of a closed pipe is given by
    \begin{equation*} f_{n}=nf_{o}\qquad \text{where}\quad n=1,3,5,\cdots. \end{equation*}
    It is observed that in case of closed pipe frequency of higher modes of vibration is odd multiples of fundamental frequency. Even harmonics are missing in this case hence sound quality is poor.
