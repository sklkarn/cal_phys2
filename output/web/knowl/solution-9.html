Solution 1.3.9.1.

Consider a charged rod of length 2a having linear charge density \(\lambda.\) To find field at a point P from the center of the rod consider a small charge element \(\,dy\) which behaves as a point charge \(\,dq\) then the field at P is given by
\begin{equation*} \,dE=\frac{k\,dq}{r^{2}} \end{equation*}
Due to charge symmetry the y components of field cancel out each other and only x components of field survived. Hence,
\begin{equation*} \,dE_{x}=\,dE\cos\theta= \frac{k\,dq}{r^{2}}\frac{x}{r} = \frac{kx\lambda\,dy}{r^{3}} =\frac{kx\lambda\,dy}{\left(x^{2}+y^{2}\right)^{3/2}} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \therefore\quad E=E_{x}=kx\lambda\int\limits_{-a}^{a}\frac{\,dy}{\left(x^{2}+y^{2}\right)^{3/2}} \end{equation*}
Now solve this integrand first then substitute the limits. Let
\begin{equation*} \mathscr{I} = \int\frac{\,dy}{\left(x^{2}+y^{2}\right)^{3/2}} \end{equation*}
From geometry of the figure
\begin{equation*} y=x\tan\theta \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,} \quad \,dy=x\sec^{2}\theta\,d\theta \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \therefore\quad \mathscr{I} = \int\frac{x\sec^{2}\theta\,d\theta}{(x^{2}+x^{2}\tan^{2}\theta)^{3/2}} = \int \frac{1}{x^{2}}\cos\theta\,d\theta = \frac{1}{x^{2}}\sin\theta=\frac{1}{x^{2}}\frac{y}{r} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} = \frac{y}{x^{2}\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \therefore\quad E= kx\lambda \left[\frac{y}{x^{2}\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}}\right]_{-a}^{a} = kx\lambda\frac{2a}{x^{2}\sqrt{x^{2}+a^{2}}} = \frac{2k\lambda a}{x \sqrt{x^{2}+a^{2}}} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} = \frac{kQ}{x \sqrt{x^{2}+a^{2}}} \end{equation*}
As total charge on a rod is Q. If \(x\gt \gt a,\) then
\begin{equation*} E= \frac{kQ}{x^{2}}, \end{equation*}
i.e., rod behaves as a point charge.
If \(a \gt \gt x \) then
\begin{equation*} E=\frac{kQ}{ax}=\frac{2k\lambda}{x} \end{equation*}
If rod is infinitely long then \(E\propto\frac{1}{x}\) as k and \(\lambda\) are constant.
Alternative in vector form:
\begin{equation*} \vec{dE}= k\frac{dq}{r^{2}}(\cos\theta\hat{i}-\sin\theta\hat{j}); \end{equation*}
Here,
\begin{equation*} \left[\cos\theta=\frac{x}{r}\right] \quad \text{ and } \quad \left[ \sin\theta=\frac{y}{r}\right] \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} also, \quad \lambda=\frac{charge}{length}=\frac{\,dq}{\,dy}=\frac{Q}{2a} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,} \quad \frac{\,dq}{Q}=\frac{\,dy}{2a}; \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \therefore, \quad \vec{E}=\int\vec{dE}; \quad \text{and} \quad r=\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}} \end{equation*}
  1. \begin{equation*} \vec{E}=\int k\frac{dq}{r^{2}}(\frac{x}{r}\hat{i}-\frac{y}{r}\hat{j}) = \int k \frac{Q}{2a}\frac{dy}{r^{3}}(x\hat{i}-y\hat{j}) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = k\frac{Qx}{2a} \int\limits_{-a}^{a} \frac{dy}{(x^{2}+y^{2})^{3/2}}\hat{i}-k\frac{Q}{2a} \int\limits_{-a}^{a} \frac{ydy}{(x^{2}+y^{2})^{3/2}}\hat{j} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = k\frac{Qx}{2a} 2 \int\limits_{0}^{a} \frac{dy}{(x^{2}+y^{2})^{3/2}}\hat{i}-0 \cdot\hat{j} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = \left. k\frac{Qx}{a}\left(\frac{y}{x^{2}\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}}}\right)\right\vert_{0}^{a}\hat{i} = k \left(\frac{Q}{x\sqrt{x^{2}+a^{2}}}\right)\hat{i} \end{equation*}
  2. \begin{equation*} \vec{E}=k\frac{Q}{x^{2}}\hat{i} \end{equation*}
    Field due to a point charge.
  3. \begin{equation*} \vec{E}=k\frac{Q}{ax}\hat{i} = \frac{1}{2\pi \epsilon_{o}}\frac{\lambda}{x}\hat{i} \end{equation*}
