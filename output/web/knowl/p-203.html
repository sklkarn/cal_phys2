Energy stored per unit volume in a capacitor is called energy density, i.e.,
\begin{equation*} u=\frac{U}{\text{Volume}}. \end{equation*}
Consider a charged conductor \(XY\) of surface charge density \(\sigma\) is placed in free space. Let a small element \(ab\) of area \(\,dA\) on the surface of the conductor and a point \(P\) just outside of it [FigureÂ 1.7.10]. Hence electric field just outside the charged conductor is given by
\begin{equation} \vec{E}=\vec{E_{1}}+\vec{E_{2}} = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_{o}} \tag{1.7.11} \end{equation}
where \(\vec{E_{1}} \) and \(\vec{E_{2}} \) are electric field at point \(P\) due to an element \(ab\) and rest of the conductor. If point \(P'\) is just inside the conductor then electric field inside the conductor is zero, i.e.,
\begin{equation*} \vec{E}=\vec{E_{1}}-\vec{E_{2}} =0 \end{equation*}
