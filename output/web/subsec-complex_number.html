    Subsection 3.9.2 The Complex Number Method for A.C. Analysis

    The complex algebra can be used to simplifies many ac problems. Let us review briefly some of the properties of complex numbers. In the expression
    \begin{equation*} z=x+jy \end{equation*}
    z is a complex quantity which has x and y as real components and
    \begin{equation*} j=\sqrt{-1} \end{equation*}
    is an imaginary quantity. Complex numbers can be represented graphically by imaging their real components as abscissas and their imaginary components as ordinates as shown in Figure¬†3.9.2. In such plot the complex quantity is represented by a vector, whose length indicates the magnitude of the complex quantity, and whose direction indicates the relative proportion of its real and imaginary constituents. The multiplication of complex vector by j is a vector operation producing a \(90^{o}\) counterclock wise rotation of the complex vector without changing its magnitude. Thus,
    \begin{equation*} jz=j(x+jy) = jx-y \end{equation*}
    has the direction in second quadrant and the vectors resulting from two further multiplications by j being in third and fourth quadrant as shown in Figure¬†3.9.2.
    The sinusoidal voltage or current can be easily represented by rotating vectors scheme. The rotating vectors are called phasors and diagram containing them are called phasor diagrams. Since the vectors are rotating in this scheme it is better to express the complex quantity in polar co-ordinate system. Suppose r is any vector making an angle \(\theta\) with the real axis then we have
    \begin{equation*} x=r\cos\theta \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} and, \quad y=r\sin\theta \end{equation*}
    Hence
    \begin{equation*} z=x+jy = r\cos\theta + j r\sin\theta = r (\cos\theta + j\sin\theta) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = r e^{j\theta} = |z|e^{j\theta} \end{equation*}
    ‚Äâ1‚Äâ where,
    \begin{equation*} r=|z|=\sqrt{x^{2}+y^{2}} \end{equation*}
    or
    \begin{equation*} |z|=\sqrt{z\cdot z^{\ast}} \end{equation*}
    is called a modulus and
    \begin{equation*} \theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{y}{x}\right) \end{equation*}
    is called the argument or phase of the complex quantity z. The sinusoidal current
    \begin{equation*} I = I_{o}\sin(\omega t+\phi) \end{equation*}
    and voltage
    \begin{equation*} E = E_{o}\sin(\omega t) \end{equation*}
    are now considered as the imaginary parts of
    \begin{equation*} I_{o}e^{j(\omega t+\phi)} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} E_{o}e^{j\omega t}, \end{equation*}
    respectively. In an expression of alternating current
    \begin{equation*} I = I_{o}e^{j(\omega t+\phi)}, \end{equation*}
    \(\phi\) is the initial phase of the current.

    Subsubsection 3.9.2.1 Impedance, Reactance, and Admittance

    Direct current in a circuit can be controlled by a resistance only, but to control alternating current resistance, inductance, and capacitance can also be used. In alternating current the ratio of potential difference across the circuit to the current flowing through it is known as impedance, Z where
    \begin{equation*} Z=\frac{E}{I}. \end{equation*}
    Physically impedance in ac (alternating current) circuit is the resistance offered by it in the flow of alternating current just as the resistance in the flow of direct current in the dc circuit. The resistance offered by the inductance and capacitance to the flow of alternating current in the ac circuit is known as the Reactance, X. The reactance due to inductance is called inductive reactance, \(X_{L}\) and that due to capacitance is called capacitive reactance, \(X_{C}\text{.}\) The inverse of the impedance is called the admittance, Y, i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} Y=\frac{1}{Z}. \end{equation*}
    Hence
    \begin{equation*} Y\cdot E = \frac{E}{Z}=I \end{equation*}

    Subsubsection 3.9.2.2 Resistance in an AC Circuit

    Consider an alternating sinusoidal voltage
    \begin{equation*} E=E_{o} \sin\omega t \end{equation*}
    an imaginary part of
    \begin{equation*} E=E_{o} e^{j\omega t} \end{equation*}
    is applied across a resistor of resistance R, [Figure¬†3.9.3.(a)] then the current flowing through it at any instant of time is given by
    \begin{equation*} I= \frac{E}{R}= \frac{E_{o} e^{j\omega t}}{R} = I_{o} e^{j\omega t}. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{where,}\quad I_{o}=\frac{E_{o}}{R} \end{equation*}
    Hence the actual current is
    \begin{equation*} I_{o} \sin\omega t \end{equation*}
    which is an imaginary part of
    \begin{equation*} I_{o} e^{j\omega t}. \end{equation*}
    Since the current and voltage rise and fall simultaneously, they are in phase with each other as shown in Figure¬†3.9.3.(b). The projection of peak voltage \(E_{o}\) and current \(I_{o}\) vectors rotating continuously in anti-clockwise direction with an angular velocity \(\omega\) [Figure¬†3.9.3.(c)] and lying completely along the x-axis at t = 0, T, 2T, etc.
    Alternative:
    \begin{equation*} E(t) = R I(t) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} E_{o}\sin\omega t = R I(t) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore I(t) = \frac{E_{o}}{R}\sin\omega t = I_{o} \sin\omega t \end{equation*}
    This implies that \(I(t)\) is in phase with \(E(t)\) [Figure¬†3.9.3.(c)].

    Subsubsection 3.9.2.3 Inductance in an AC Circuit

    Consider an alternating emf
    \begin{equation*} E=E_{o} e^{j\omega t} \end{equation*}
    is applied across an inductor of inductance L, and I be the current flowing through it then from the circuit [Figure¬†3.9.4.(a)] we have
    \begin{equation*} E = E_{o} e^{j\omega t}= L\frac{\,dI}{\,dt} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \,dI = \frac{E_{o}}{L} e^{j\omega t}\,dt \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad I=\int\,dI = \frac{E_{o}}{j\omega L} e^{j\omega t} = \frac{E}{j\omega L} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad I = \frac{E}{j\omega L}\times\frac{j}{j}=- \frac{jE}{\omega L} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =-\frac{jE_{o}e^{j\omega t}}{\omega L} = \frac{E_{o}e^{j\omega t}e^{-j\frac{\pi}{2}}}{\omega L} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = \frac{E_{o}e^{j(\omega t-\frac{\pi}{2})}}{\omega L} = I_{o} e^{j(\omega t-\frac{\pi}{2})} \end{equation*}
    \(\left\lbrace\because -j=\cos\frac{\pi}{2}-j\sin\frac{\pi}{2}\right\rbrace\)
    \begin{equation*} \text{here}\quad I_{o}= \frac{E_{o}}{\omega L} \end{equation*}
    is the amplitude of actual current,
    \begin{equation*} I=I_{o}\sin \left(\omega t -\frac{\pi}{2}\right). \end{equation*}
    This expression indicates that the current through the inductor is also alternating but lagging behind the emf by \(\frac{\pi}{2}\) or with \(90^{o}\) phase difference. In
    \begin{equation*} I_{o}= \frac{E_{o}}{\omega L} \end{equation*}
    the quantity \(\omega L \) has the dimension of reactance \(X_{L}.\) The reciprocal of inductive reactance is called inductive susceptance which is \(\frac{1}{\omega L}.\) The inductive reactance \(X_{L}\) measures the hindrance to the flow of ac just like resistance R in purely resistive circuit. Unlike resistances the average power consumed by inductance is zero. In an expression
    \begin{equation*} I=\frac{E}{j\omega L} \end{equation*}
    the quantity \(j\omega L\) is the total resistance in an ac circuit hence it is called the impedance Z of the inductance. That is
    \begin{equation*} X_{L}=\omega L \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{and}\quad Z=j\omega L \end{equation*}
    Alternative:
    \begin{equation*} E(t) = L \frac{\,dI(t)}{\,dt} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or,\quad \int_{0}^{t} E_{o}\sin\omega t \,dt= L \int_{0}^{I}\,dI(t) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore I(t) = -\frac{E_{o}}{\omega L}\cos\omega t = I_{o} \sin\left(\omega t-\frac{\pi}{2}\right) \end{equation*}
    This implies that \(I(t)\) is lagging behind \(E(t)\) by phase \(\pi/2.\)

    Subsubsection 3.9.2.4 Capacitance in an AC Circuit

    Consider an alternating emf
    \begin{equation*} E=E_{o} e^{j\omega t} \end{equation*}
    is applied across a capacitor of capacitance C, and I be the current flowing through it then from the circuit [Figure¬†3.9.5.(a)] we have
    \begin{equation*} E_{o}e^{j\omega t} = \frac{q}{C} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{But,}\quad I=\frac{\,dq}{\,dt} = \frac{\,d}{\,dt}(CE_{o}e^{j\omega t})=j\omega CE_{o}e^{j\omega t} =\frac{E}{j\omega C} \end{equation*}
    \(\left\lbrace \because j=e^{j\frac{\pi}{2}}=\cos\frac{\pi}{2}+j\sin\frac{\pi}{2} \right\rbrace \)
    We can write
    \begin{equation*} I = \omega C E_{o}e^{j\omega t}e^{j\frac{\pi}{2}} = \omega C E_{o}e^{j(\omega t+\frac{\pi}{2})} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = \frac{E_{o}}{1/\omega C}e^{j(\omega t+\frac{\pi}{2})}= I_{o}e^{j(\omega t+\frac{\pi}{2})} \end{equation*}
    where,
    \begin{equation*} I_{o}= \frac{E_{o}}{1/\omega C} \end{equation*}
    is the peak value of altenating current,
    \begin{equation*} I=I_{o}\sin\left(\omega t+\frac{\pi}{2}\right) \end{equation*}
    Hence, in this case also current is alternating sinusoidal with frequency \(\omega\) but leading the emf by \(\frac{\pi}{2}\) phase as shown in Figure¬†3.9.5.(b).
    In this case, the capacitive reactance
    \begin{equation*} X_{C} = \frac{E_{o}}{I_{o}}= \frac{1}{\omega C} \end{equation*}
    and impedance,
    \begin{equation*} Z = \frac{E}{I}=\frac{1}{j\omega C}. \end{equation*}
    The capacitive reactance \(X_{C}\) measures the hindrance to the flow of ac just like resistance R in purely resistive circuit. The reciprocal of capacitive reactance is called capacitive susceptance which is \(\omega C\text{.}\) Even though capacitor provide hindrance to the flow of current it does not consume any energy over a cycle.
    Alternative:
    \begin{equation*} E(t) = \frac{q(t)}{C} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} but, \quad I(t) = \frac{\,dq(t)}{\,dt} = \frac{\,d}{\,dt}\left(CE_{o}\sin\omega t\right)= C\omega E_{o}\cos\omega t \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore I(t) = I_{o} \sin\left(\omega t+\frac{\pi}{2}\right) \end{equation*}
    This implies that \(I(t)\) is leading \(E(t)\) by phase \(\pi/2\) [Figure¬†3.9.5.(c)].

    Subsubsection 3.9.2.6 Power in an AC Circuit

    Power is dc circuit is given by
    \begin{equation*} P=VI \end{equation*}
    as voltage and current in dc circuit is constant but in ac circuit they are changing with time hence in ac circuit instantaneous power dissipated can be determined as
    \begin{equation*} P = VI = EI = E_{o}\sin(\omega t)\times I_{o}\sin(\omega t+\theta) \end{equation*}
    where \(\theta\) is a phase difference between current and voltage.
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad P = E_{o}I_{o}\sin(\omega t)\sin(\omega t+\theta) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = \frac{E_{o}I_{o}}{2} \left[\cos(\omega t - \omega t - \theta)-\cos(\omega t+\omega t+\theta)\right] \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad P = \frac{E_{o}I_{o}}{2} \left[\cos(- \theta)-\cos(2\omega t+\theta)\right] \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad P =\frac{E_{o}I_{o}}{2}\left[\cos(\theta)-\cos(2\omega t+\theta)\right] \end{equation*}
    \(\because\quad \cos(-\theta) =\cos\theta ;\) \(\sin\,A\sin\,B=\frac{1}{2}\left[\cos\,(A-B)-\cos\,(A+B)\right].\)
    Therefore average power dissipated over a cycle is given by
    \begin{equation*} P_{av}=\frac{\int\limits_{0}^{T}P\,dt}{\int\limits_{0}^{T}\,dt} = \frac{1}{T}\int\limits_{0}^{T}P\,dt \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad P_{av}=\frac{1}{T}\int\limits_{0}^{T}\frac{E_{o}I_{o}}{2}\left[\cos(\theta)-\cos(2\omega t+\theta)\right]\,dt \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\frac{E_{o}I_{o}}{2}\cos\theta = \left(\frac{E_{o}}{\sqrt{2}}\right)\left(\frac{I_{o}}{\sqrt{2}}\right)\cos\theta \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} [\therefore \quad P_{av} = E_{rms}I_{rms}\cos\theta \end{equation*}
    Hence if current and voltage is \(90^{o} \) out of phase then \(\theta= 90^{o} \) and average power consumption in ac circuit is
    \begin{equation*} [P_{av} = E_{rms}I_{rms}\cos 90^{o} = 0] \end{equation*}
    zero. Here \(\cos\theta\) is called a Power Factor which can be defined as \(\cos\theta = \frac{R}{Z}.\)

    Subsubsection 3.9.2.7 AC Circuit with Resistance and Inductance in Series

    Let emf of
    \begin{equation*} E=E_{o}e^{j\omega t} \end{equation*}
    be applied to a circuit containing a resistor R and an inductor L in series as shown in Figure¬†3.9.7, then from Kirchhoff‚Äôs law, we have
    \begin{equation*} E-E_{L}=E_{R} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad E = RI+j\omega L I \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad I = \frac{E}{R+j\omega L} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{But,}\quad I = \frac{E}{Z}, \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore \quad Z=R+j\omega L, \end{equation*}
    which is a complex quantity.
    \begin{equation*} \text{Again,}\hspace {2pt} Z= |Z| e^{j\theta} = \sqrt{R^{2}+\omega^{2}L^{2}} e^{j\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{\omega L}{R}\right)}; \end{equation*}
    see Figure¬†3.9.8.(c).
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad I = \frac{E_{o}e^{j\omega t}}{\sqrt{R^{2}+\omega^{2}L^{2}} e^{j\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{\omega L}{R}\right)}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = \frac{E_{o}}{ \sqrt{R^{2}+\omega^{2}L^{2}}}e^{j(\omega t-\phi}); \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{where, } \phi = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{\omega L}{R}\right); \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{Hence,}\quad I = I_{o}e^{j(\omega t-\phi)} \end{equation*}
    Here modulus of impedance Z is
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt{R^{2}+\omega^{2}L^{2}} = \sqrt{R^{2}+X^{2}_{L}} \end{equation*}
    and current I is lagging behind the emf E by an angle
    \begin{equation*} \phi = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{\omega L}{R}\right) \end{equation*}
    [Figure¬†3.9.8.(b)] lying between \(0^{o}\) and \(90^{o}\) as shown in Figure¬†3.9.8.(a).

    Subsubsection 3.9.2.8 AC Circuit with Resistance and Capacitance in Series

    Let emf of
    \begin{equation*} E=E_{o}e^{j\omega t} \end{equation*}
    be applied to a circuit containing a resistor of resistance R and a capacitor of capacitance C in series as shown in Figure¬†3.9.9, then from Kirchhoff‚Äôs law, we have
    \begin{equation*} E=E_{C}+E_{R}; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad E = RI+\frac{1}{j\omega C}I \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad I = \frac{E}{R+\frac{1}{j\omega C}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{But,}\quad I = \frac{E}{Z} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore \quad Z = R+\frac{1}{j\omega C} = R-\frac{j}{\omega C} \end{equation*}
    which is complex quantity.
    \begin{equation*} \text{Again}\quad Z = |Z| e^{j\theta} = \sqrt{\left(R^{2}+\frac{1}{\omega^{2}C^{2}}\right)} e^{-j\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{1/\omega C}{R}\right)} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad I = \frac{E_{o} e^{j\omega t}}{\sqrt{\left(R^{2}+\frac{1}{\omega^{2}C^{2}}\right)} e^{-j\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{1/\omega C}{R}\right)}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = \frac{E_{o}}{ \sqrt{\left(R^{2}+\frac{1}{\omega^{2}C^{2}}\right)}}e^{j(\omega t+\phi)} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{where }\quad \phi=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{1/\omega C}{R}\right)=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{\omega CR}\right) \end{equation*}
    The current in this circuit leads the applied emf by an angle \(\phi\) between \(0^{o}\) and \(90^{o}\) as shown in Figure¬†3.9.10.(a). The capacitive reactance
    \begin{equation*} X_{C}=\frac{1}{\omega C} \end{equation*}
    lags behind the resistance R by an angle \(90^{o}\) as shown in Figure¬†3.9.10.(b).

    Subsubsection 3.9.2.9 AC Circuit with Resistance, Capacitance, and Inductance in Series

    Let emf of \(E=E_{o}e^{j\omega t}\) be applied to a circuit containing a resistor of resistance R, an inductor of inductance L, and a capacitor of capacitance C in series as shown in Figure¬†3.9.11.(a), then from Kirchhoff‚Äôs law, we have
    \begin{equation*} E=V_{R}+V_{L}+V_{C}; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad E=RI+j\omega LI + \frac{1}{j\omega C}I \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad E=\left(R+j\omega L + \frac{1}{j\omega C}\right)I; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad E= \left[R+j\omega L - \frac{j}{\omega C}\right]I \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad I = \frac{E}{Z}= \frac{E}{\left[R+j\left(\omega L - \frac{1}{\omega C}\right)\right]} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore \quad Z= \left[R+j\left(\omega L - \frac{1}{\omega C}\right)\right] = \left[R-j\left( \frac{1}{\omega C}-\omega L \right)\right] \end{equation*}
    Minus sign between \(X_{L}=\omega L\) and \(X_{C}= \frac{1}{\omega C}\) indicates that they are in opposite phase to each other. The circuit becomes inductive, capacitive or purely resistive if \(X_{L} \gt X_{C},\) \(X_{L} \lt X_{C},\) or \(X_{L} = X_{C}.\) Now since impedance \(Z=|Z|e^{j\theta}\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} |Z|= \sqrt{R^{2}+\left(\frac{1}{\omega C}-\omega L \right)^{2}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{and}\quad \theta =-\tan^{-1}\left[\frac{\left(\frac{1}{\omega C}-\omega L\right)}{R} \right] \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore \quad Z=\sqrt{R^{2}+\left(\frac{1}{\omega C}-\omega L \right)^{2}} e^{-j\tan^{-1}\left[\frac{\left(\frac{1}{\omega C}-\omega L\right)}{R} \right]} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{Hence,}\quad I= \frac{E_{o}e^{j\omega t}}{\sqrt{R^{2}+\left(\frac{1}{\omega C}-\omega L \right)^{2}} e^{-j\tan^{-1}\left[\frac{\left(\frac{1}{\omega C}-\omega L\right)}{R} \right]}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = \frac{E_{o}e^{j(\omega t+\phi)}} {\sqrt{R^{2}+\left(\frac{1}{\omega C}-\omega L \right)^{2}}} \end{equation*}
    where phase angle
    \begin{equation*} \phi = -\tan^{-1}\left[\frac{\left(\frac{1}{\omega C}-\omega L\right)}{R} \right] \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} E_{R}=RI = RI_{o}e^{j(\omega t +\phi)} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} E_{L}=j\omega L I = j\omega L I_{o}e^{j(\omega t +\phi)} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} E_{C}=-j\frac{1}{\omega C}I = -j \frac{1}{\omega C} I_{o}e^{j(\omega t +\phi)} \end{equation*}

    Subsubsection 3.9.2.10 AC Circuit with Resistance, Capacitance, and Inductance in Parallel

    Let emf \(E=E_{o}\sin\omega t \) be applied to a circuit containing a resistor of resistance R, an inductor of inductance L, and a capacitor of capacitance C in parallel as shown in Figure¬†3.9.12.(a). Hence,
    \begin{equation*} E(t) = V_{R}=V_{L}=V_{C} \end{equation*}
    Now,
    \begin{equation*} I_{R}(t)=\frac{E(t)}{R} = \frac{E_{o}}{R} \sin\omega t = I_{Ro} \sin\omega t \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} V_{L}(t)=E(t)=E_{o}\sin\omega t = L\frac{\,dI_{L}}{\,dt} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore I_{L}(t)= \int_{0}^{t} \frac{E_{o}}{L}\sin\omega t'\,dt' \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =-\frac{E_{o}}{\omega L}\cos\omega t = \frac{E_{o}}{X_{L}}\sin\left(\omega t-\frac{\pi}{2}\right) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =I_{Lo} \sin\left(\omega t-\frac{\pi}{2}\right) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} V_{C}(t)=E(t)=E_{o}\sin\omega t = \frac{Q(t)}{C} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore I_{C}(t)= \frac{\,dQ(t)}{\,dt} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\omega CE_{o}\cos\omega t = X_{C}E_{o}\sin\left(\omega t+\frac{\pi}{2}\right) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =I_{Co} \sin\left(\omega t+\frac{\pi}{2}\right) \end{equation*}
    Using Kirchoff‚Äôs current law at the junction, we have
    \begin{equation*} I(t) = I_{R}(t)+I_{L}(t)+I_{C}(t) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = I_{Ro}\sin\left(\omega t\right)+I_{Lo} \sin\left(\omega t-\frac{\pi}{2}\right)+I_{Co} \sin\left(\omega t+\frac{\pi}{2}\right) \end{equation*}
    From phasor,
    \begin{equation*} \vec{I}_{o}= \vec{I}_{Ro}+\vec{I}_{Co}+\vec{I}_{Lo} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore I_{o} = \sqrt{I^{2}_{Ro} + \left(I_{Co}-I_{Lo}\right)^{2}} = E_{o}\sqrt{\frac{1}{R^{2}}+\left(X_{C}+\frac{1}{X_{L}}\right)^{2}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore I_{o}=\frac{E_{o}}{Z} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore \frac{1}{Z}=\sqrt{\frac{1}{R^{2}}+\left(\omega C-\frac{1}{\omega L}\right)^{2}} \end{equation*}
    At resonant condition, \(\theta = 0\)
    \begin{equation*} \therefore X_{C}=X_{L} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \omega_{o}C=\frac{1}{\omega_{o}L} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or, \quad \omega_{o} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}} \end{equation*}
