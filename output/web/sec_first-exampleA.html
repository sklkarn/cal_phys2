    Section 1.3 Examples A

    Coulomb‚Äôs Law.

    Example 1.3.1.

    If \(3.2\times10^{20}\) electrons move through a cigarette lighter during its operation, how many coulombs of charge moved through it?
    Solution.
    \begin{equation*} Q = N_{e}q_{e} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = (3.2\times10^{20})\times(1.6\times10^{-19}) = 51.2 \,C \end{equation*}

    Example 1.3.2.

    How many coulombs of positive charge are there in 1.00 kg of plutonium? Given: atomic mass of plutonium atom is 244 and each plutonium atom has 94 protons.
    Solution.
    From eqn. (1.0.1)
    \begin{equation*} Q = N_{e}q_{e} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or, \quad Q= (N_{atom})\times (Z) \times(q_{e}) \end{equation*}
    where \(N_{atom}\) is number of atoms and \(Z\) is atomic number.
    \begin{equation*} = n \times N_{A} \times Z \times q_{e} \end{equation*}
    where \(n\) is number of moles and \(N_{A}\) is Avagadro‚Äôs number.
    = [(Mass of material, m)/(Molar Mass, M)](Avagadro‚Äôs number, \(N_{A}\)) (Atomic Number, Z) (Charge on electron, \(q_{e}\))
    \begin{equation*} or, \quad Q = \frac{m}{M}\times (N_{A})\times Z\times q_{e} \end{equation*}
    Here \(Q\) is total charge in material, \(M\) is mass number, in case of elemental material value of mass number, A is same as molar mass, M.
    \begin{equation*} \therefore \quad Q_{+} = \frac{1000\,g}{244\,g/mol}\times (6.02\times 10^{23})(\#/mol)\times 94 \times (1.6\times 10^{-19}\,C) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =3.7\times10^{7}\,C \end{equation*}

    Example 1.3.3.

    A hydrogen atom consists of a proton and an electron. If the radius of the atom is equal to the Bohr radius of \(0.5292 \times 10^{-10} \,m\text{,}\) what is
    1. the gravitational force between the two particles,
    2. the electrical force between the particles, and
    3. the ratio of the electrical force to the gravitational force?
    Note: Newton‚Äôs gravitational constant, \(G = 6.67\times 10^{-11} \,N m^{2}/kg^{2};\) Coulomb‚Äôs Electrostatic constant, \(k = 9.00 \times 10^{9} \,N m^{2}/C^{2};\) mass of proton, \(m_{p} = 1.67 \times 10^{-27} \,kg;\) mass of electron, \(m_{e} = 9.11\times 10^{-31} \,kg;\) charge of proton, \(q_{p} = 1.60 \times 10^{-19} \,C;\) and charge of electron, \(q_{e} = -1.60 \times 10^{-19} \,C.\)
    Solution.
    Given: Radius of atom, \(r = 0.5292 \times 10^{-10} \,m.\)
    1. From Newton‚Äôs law of gravity,
      \begin{equation*} F_{g} = G\frac{m_{p}m_{e}}{r^{2}} = 3.6\times10^{-47} \,N \end{equation*}
    2. From Coulomb‚Äôs law of electrostatics,
      \begin{equation*} F_{e} = k\frac{q_{p}q_{e}}{r^{2}} = 8.23\times10^{-8}\,N \end{equation*}
    3. Ratio of two forces,
      \begin{equation*} R = \frac{F_{e}}{F_{g}}= 2.27\times 10^{39} \end{equation*}

    Example 1.3.4.

    Three charges are located along the x-axis. The first charge of \(+1 \,\mu C \) is located at \(x = 0,\) the second of \(- 2 \,\mu C\) is at x = 5 cm, and the third charge of \(+3 \,\mu C \) is located in such way that there is no electrical force on it.
    1. Where is the third charge located?
    2. What is the electrical force on the first charge?
    3. What is the electrical force on the second charge?
    Solution.
    Given: \(q_{1} = 1.00 \times 10^{-6} \,C; \quad q_{2} = -2.00 \times 10^{-6} \,C;\) \(q_{3} = 3.00 \times10^{-6} \,C; \) \(k = 9.00 \times 10^{9} \,N m^{2}/C^{2};\) \(r_{12} = 5 \times 10^{-2} \,m.\)
    1. Let the charges are located in a line as shown in figure below, then magnitude of force on \(3^{rd}\) charge due to \(1^{st}\) and \(2^{nd}\) charges is given by
      \begin{equation*} |\vec{F}_{31}| = k\frac{q_{1}q_{3}}{r_{13}^{2}}; \end{equation*}
      and
      \begin{equation*} |\vec{F}_{32}| = k\frac{q_{2}q_{3}}{r_{23}^{2}} \end{equation*}
      Since there is not net force acting on \(3^{rd} \) charge, [figure (a)] hence
      \begin{equation*} \vec{F}=\vec{F}_{31} + \vec{F}_{32} = 0 \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad k\frac{q_{1}q_{3}}{r_{13}^{2}} + k\frac{q_{2}q_{3}}{r_{23}^{2}} =0 \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \frac{q_{1}}{r_{13}^{2}} = - \frac{q_{2}}{r_{23}^{2}} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \frac{ 1.00 \times 10^{-6}}{x^{2}} = \frac{ 2.00 \times 10^{-6} }{(r_{12}+ x)^{2}} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \frac{ 1}{x^{2}} = \frac{ 2}{(5 + x)^{2}} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad 2x^{2} = x^{2}+10x+25 \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad x^{2} - 10x-25 =0 \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad x = \frac{10 \pm \sqrt{100+4\cdot1\cdot25}}{2\cdot1} = 12.1 \,cm, -2.1 \,cm \end{equation*}
      Here we have taken x = -2.1 cm to obtain no net force on the \(3^{rd}\) charge. Hence x=\(r_{13}\)=-2.1 cm = -0.02 m.
    2. \begin{equation*} \vec{F}_{1} = \vec{F}_{12} + \vec{F}_{13} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} = k\frac{q_{1}q_{2}}{r^{2}_{12}} (\hat{i})+k\frac{q_{1}q_{3}}{r^{2}_{13}} (\hat{i}) \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} = 9\times 10^{-3}(800 + 7500)(\hat{i}) = 74.7 (\hat{i}) N \end{equation*}
      here, \(\hat{i}\) is a unit vector along x-axis [figure (b)]. Here only magnitude of charges are taken in calculation as the direction of forces are already determined according to nature of charges.
    3. \begin{equation*} \vec{F}_{2} = \vec{F}_{21} + \vec{F}_{23} = 18.2 (-\hat{i}) N \end{equation*}
      [figure (c)]

    Example 1.3.5.

    Consider the three charges \(1 \,\mu C,\) \(2 \,\mu C\) and \(-3 \,\mu C\) located at the three corners of a square. What is the magnitude and direction of the initial acceleration of the \(4 \,\mu C\) charge at the fourth corner if mass of all the charged objects are 2 kg each and are released from rest? Neglect gravity.
    Solution.
    Given: \(q_{1} = 1 \times 10^{-6} \,C; \quad k = 9.00\times 10^{9} \,N m^{2}/C^{2};\) \(q_{2} = 2 \times 10^{-6}\,C; \quad r_{2} = 0.1 \,m;\) \(q_{3} = -3 \times 10^{-6}\,C;\) \(r_{3} = 0.1 \,m;\quad q_{4} = 4 \times 10^{-6}\,C;\quad m = 2 \,kg;\)
    The direction of force \(F_{41}\) can be determined by
    \begin{equation*} \tan\theta = \frac{r_{24}}{r_{12}} \Rightarrow \quad \theta= 45\,^{o} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{Now,}\quad r_{14} = \sqrt{r^{2}_{12}+r^{2}_{24}} = 0.1414\, m \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad F_{41} = k\frac{q_{1}q_{4}}{r^{2}_{14}} =1.80 \,N; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} F_{42} = k\frac{q_{2}q_{4}}{r^{2}_{24}} =7.20 \,N; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{and}\quad F_{43} = k\frac{q_{3}q_{4}}{r^{2}_{34}}= -10.80 \,N \end{equation*}
    Therefore force along x-axis on charge \(q_{4}\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} \sum F_{x} = F_{41}\cos\theta -F_{43} = ma_{x} \Rightarrow \quad a_{x} = -4.76 \,m/s^{2} \end{equation*}
    and along y-axis is
    \begin{equation*} \sum F_{y} = F_{42} + F_{41}\sin\theta =ma_{y} \Rightarrow \quad a_{y} = 4.235 \,m/s^{2} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{Hence}\quad a = \sqrt{a^{2}_{x}+a^{2}_{y}} =6.45 \,m/s^{2} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{and}\quad \tan\phi = \frac{a_{y}}{a_{x}} \end{equation*}
    Therefore, \(\phi =41^{o} \) or, \(139^{o} \) from +ve x-axis.

    Example 1.3.6.

    A thin ring of charge Q and radius a is centered on the x-axis in the y, z plane as shown in figure. A point charge q is located a distance x from the center of the ring.
    1. What is the electrical force exerted by the ring on the point charge?
    2. What is this force equal to when the point charge is at the center of the ring?
    3. What is this force equal to when x is much greater than a?
    4. What is this force when \(Q = 10 \,\mu C, \quad q = -5 \,\mu C, \) a = 0.1 m, and x = 0.2 m?
    Solution.
    Given: \(\epsilon_{o}=8.85\times 10^{-12}\,C^{2}/Nm^{2};\) \(Q=10\times 10^{-6}\,C;\) \(q= -5\times 10^{-6}\,C;\) a = 0.1 m, x = 0.2 m.
    Consider a differential charge \(\,dQ\) at the top of a ring of length \(\,ds\) so that it behaves like a point charge to obtain a force acting on charge q at a distance x from the ring. Hence,
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\,dQ}{Q} = \frac{\,ds}{2\pi a} \end{equation*}
    as the charges are distributed uniformly on a ring.
    \begin{equation*} \therefore \quad \,dQ = \frac{Q}{2\pi a}\,ds \end{equation*}
    where \(2\pi a\) = length of a ring.
    \begin{equation*} \text{Now,}\quad \,dF=k\frac{\,dQ q}{r^{2}}, \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{where}\quad r=\sqrt{a^{2}+x^{2}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad \,dF_{x} = dF\cos\theta = k\frac{\,dQ \,q}{r^{2}}\left(\frac{x}{r}\right) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = k\frac{\,dQ \,q}{r^{3}}x = k\frac{\,dQ \,q x}{(a^{2}+x^{2})^{3/2}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{also,}\quad \,dF_{y} = dF\sin\theta = k\frac{\,dQ \,q}{r^{2}}\left( \frac{a}{r}\right) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = k\frac{\,dQ \,q}{r^{3}}a = k\frac{\,dQ \,q a}{(a^{2}+x^{2})^{3/2}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad F_{x}=\int \,dF_{x} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = k\frac{q x}{(a^{2}+x^{2})^{3/2}} \int \,dQ =k\frac{Q \,q \,x}{(a^{2}+x^{2})^{3/2}} \end{equation*}
    If we consider a differential charge \(\,dQ \) at the bottom, then y-component of force cancels out each other at a charge q and hence similarly all the y-components cancel out each other if we consider a differential charge at any portion of the ring because of the charge symmetry, i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} F_{y}=\int \,dF_{y}= 0 \end{equation*}
    by charge symmetry.
    1. \begin{equation*} \vec{F}=F_{x}\hat{i}+F_{y}\hat{j} = k\frac{Q \,q x}{(a^{2}+x^{2})^{3/2}}\hat{i} \end{equation*}
    2. \(\displaystyle \text{For} \quad x=0, \quad F = 0\)
    3. \(\text{For}\quad x \gt \gt a,\) we have \(\quad 1 \gt \gt \frac{a}{x},\)
      \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad \vec{F}= k\frac{Q \,q \,x}{x^{3}(\frac{a^{2}}{x^{2}}+1)^{3/2}} \hat{i}= k\frac{Q \,q}{x^{2}}\hat{i} \end{equation*}
      which is Coulomb‚Äôs law for point charges.
    4. \(\displaystyle F=-8.05 \,N\hat{i}\)

    Electric Field.

    Example 1.3.7.

    An electric dipole has a negative charge of -10 nC at point (-1.0 m, 0) and a positive charge of +10 nC at point (+1.0 m, 0). What is its electric field at points
    1. (0,0),
    2. (2 m,0)
    3. (0,1 m),
    4. (2 m,-1 m)
    5. What is the dipole moment of this dipole?
    Solution.
    Given: \(q = 10.0\times 10^{-9} \,C; \quad d = 2.0 \,m; \) \(k = 9.0\times10^{9} \,N.m^{2}/C^{2}\)
    1. \begin{equation*} \vec{E_{a}}=\vec{E_{1a}}+\vec{E_{2a}} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \vec{E_{1a}} =k\frac{q}{r^{2}_{1a}}(-\hat{i}),\quad r_{1a} =1\,m; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \vec{E_{2a}} =k\frac{-q}{r^{2}_{2a}}(\hat{i}),\quad r_{2a} =1\,m \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad \vec{E_{a}}=-180 (N/C) \hat{i} \end{equation*}
    2. \begin{equation*} \vec{E_{b}}=\vec{E_{1b}}+\vec{E_{2b}} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \vec{E_{1b}} =k\frac{q}{r^{2}_{1b}}\hat{i},\quad r_{1a} =1\,m; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \vec{E_{2b}} =k\frac{-q}{r^{2}_{2b}}\hat{i},\quad r_{2b} =3\,m \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad \vec{E_{b}}=8 (N/C) \hat{i} \end{equation*}
    3. \begin{equation*} \vec{E_{c}}=\vec{E_{1c}}+\vec{E_{2c}} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \vec{E_{1c}} =k\frac{q}{r^{2}_{1c}}\left(\frac{-\hat{i}+\hat{j}}{\sqrt{2}}\right), \quad r_{1c} =\sqrt{2}\,m; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \vec{E_{2c}} =k\frac{-q}{r^{2}_{2c}}\left(\frac{\hat{i}+\hat{j}}{\sqrt{2}}\right), \quad r_{2c} =\sqrt{2}\,m \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad \vec{E_{c}}=-63.6 (N/C) \hat{i} \end{equation*}
    4. \begin{equation*} \vec{E_{d}}=\vec{E_{1d}}+\vec{E_{2d}} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \vec{E_{1c}} =k\frac{q}{r^{2}_{1d}}\left(\frac{\hat{i}-\hat{j}}{\sqrt{1^{2}+1^{2}}}\right), \quad r_{1d} = \sqrt{2}\,m; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \vec{E_{2d}} =k\frac{-q}{r^{2}_{2d}}\left(\frac{3\hat{i}-\hat{j}}{\sqrt{3^{2}+1^{2}}}\right), \quad r_{2d} =\sqrt{10}\,m \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad \vec{E_{d}}=23.3 (N/C) \hat{i} -29.0 (N/C) \hat{j} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} = 37.2 (N/C) \left(\frac{23.3\hat{i}-29.0\hat{j}}{37.2}\right) \end{equation*}
    5. \begin{equation*} \vec{p}=qd\hat{i} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad \vec{p}=2.0\times 10^{-8}(Cm) \hat{i} \end{equation*}

    Example 1.3.8.

    A water molecule has an oxygen ion of charge -2 e at the origin of a coordinate system and two hydrogen ions of charge +1 e at a distance of \(4.00\times 10^{-11} \,m\) making angles of \(\pm 60^{o}\) as shown in the figure below. The molecule is in an external electric field of strength 100 N/C directed above the x-axis at an angle of \(45^{o}.\)
    1. What is the dipole moment of the molecule?
    2. What is the electrical force on the oxygen molecule due to the external field.
    3. What is the torque on the molecule?
    4. What is the potential energy due to the dipole moment of the molecule?
    Solution.
    Given: \(q = 2 e; \quad e = 1.60\times 10^{-19} \,C; \quad d = 4.00\times 10^{-11} \,m; \) \(\theta = 60^{o}; \quad \phi = 45^{o}; \quad E= 100 (N/C).\)
    1. \begin{equation*} \vec{p}=\vec{p_{1}}+\vec{p_{2}} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \vec{p_{1}} = e d(\cos\theta\hat{i}+\sin\theta \hat{j}); \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \vec{p_{2}} = e d(\cos\theta\hat{i}-\sin\theta \hat{j}); \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \vec{p}=2e d\cos\theta \hat{i}=6.40\times 10^{-30}\,Cm \hat{i} \end{equation*}
    2. \begin{equation*} F = q\vec{E} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \vec{E} = E(\cos\phi\hat{i}+\sin\phi \hat{j}); \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \vec{F} = 2.26\times 10^{-17}\,N(\hat{i}+ \hat{j}) \end{equation*}
    3. \begin{equation*} \tau=\vec{p}\times\vec{E} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \vec{p}=p\hat{i}; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \vec{E}=E\cos\phi\hat{i}+E\sin\phi \hat{j}; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \tau= \begin{bmatrix} \hat{i} & \hat{j} & \hat{k}\\ p & 0 & 0 \\ E \cos \phi & E\sin \phi & 0 \end{bmatrix} = pE\sin\phi \hat{k}=4.53\times 10^{-28} \,Nm\hat{k} \end{equation*}
    4. \begin{equation*} U = -\vec{p}\cdot\vec{E} = -pE = -4.53\times 10^{-28} \,J \end{equation*}
