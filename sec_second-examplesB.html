    DC Circuits.

    Example 2.5.1.

    A 12 V battery with an internal resistance of 1 ohm is connected to four other resistors as shown in figure below. Find
    1. the voltage across and the current through each resistor.
    2. the voltage \(V_{ab}\) across the battery, and
    3. the the electrical potential at point c.
    Provided: \(R_{1} =1 \,\Omega; \quad R_{2}= 2\,\Omega;\) \(R_{3}= 3\,\Omega; \quad R_{4}= 6\,\Omega; \) and \(E = 12\,V\)
    Solution.
    (Find equivalent resistance and work backward to get voltage and current.)
    Given: \(r= 1\,\Omega;\quad R_{1} =1 \,\Omega; \quad R_{2}= 2\Omega;\) \(R_{3}= 6\,\Omega;\quad R_{4}= 3\,\Omega;\quad E = 12\,V\)
    Since \(R_{1}\) and \(R_{2}\) are in series, we have
    \begin{equation*} R_{12} = R_{1}+R_{2} = 3 \,\Omega; \end{equation*}
    Since \(R_{12}\) and \(R_{3}\) are in parallel, we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{R_{123}}= \frac{1}{R_{12}} + \frac{1}{R_{3}}; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore R_{123} = 2 \,\Omega \end{equation*}
    Since \(r, \quad R_{123},\) and \(R_{4}\) are in series, we have
    \begin{equation*} R_{1234}= r + R_{123} + R_{4} = 6 \,\Omega \end{equation*}
    Now, from figure below, we have
    \begin{equation*} \left[ V = I R \right]; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or, \quad I = \frac{V}{R} = \frac{E}{R_{1234}} = 12/6 = 2\,A \end{equation*}
    Moving backward: from figure
    \begin{equation*} V_{o} = I r =2\,A\cdot1\,\Omega = 2\,V; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{ also,} \quad V_{4} = I R_{4}= 2\,A\cdot3\,\Omega = 6\,V \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{and} \quad V_{123} = I R_{123}= 2\,A\cdot2\,\Omega = 4\,V \end{equation*}
    from figure
    \begin{equation*} \because \quad V_{12} = I_{12} R_{12}; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore I_{12} = \frac{V_{12}}{R_{12}} = \frac{V_{123}}{R_{12}} = 1.33 \,A \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{also,} \quad \because \quad V_{3} = I_{3} R_{3}; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore I_{3} = \frac{V_{3}}{R_{3}} = \frac{V_{123}}{R_{3}} = 0.67 \,A \end{equation*}
    from figure
    \begin{equation*} \because \quad V_{1} = I_{1} R_{1} = I_{12} R_{1} = 1.33\,A\cdot 1\,\Omega = 1.33 \,V \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{also,}\quad \because \quad V_{2} = I_{12} R_{2}=1.33\,A\cdot 2\,\Omega = 2.67\, V \end{equation*}
    1. \begin{equation*} V_{o}=2\,V; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} I_{o}=2\,A; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} V_{1}=1.33\,V; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} I_{1}=1.33\,A; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} V_{2}=2.67\,V; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} I_{2}=1.33\,A; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} V_{4}=6\,V; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} I_{4}=2\,A; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} V_{3}=4\,V; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} I_{3}=0.67\,A \end{equation*}
    2. \begin{equation*} V_{ab}=E - V_{o}= 10\,V \end{equation*}
    3. \begin{equation*} V_{c}= V_{cb}=V_{2}+V_{4} = 8.67\,V \end{equation*}

    Example 2.5.2.

    In figures below the emf of the battery is 6 V, the internal resistance is \(10 \,\Omega,\) and the load resistance is \(4 \,k\Omega.\) Find the readings of the ammeter and the voltmeter in each of the three cases.
    Solution.
    1. For first figure
      \begin{equation*} E = 6 \,V;\quad r = 10 \,\Omega;\quad R= 4000 \,\Omega \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} R_{T} = r +R; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} V_{T} = I R_{T}; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} V = I R \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} R_{T} = 4010 \,\Omega; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} I = 1.496 \,mA; \end{equation*}
      Hence, Ammeter reads 1.496 mA.
      \begin{equation*} V = 5.985 \,V; \end{equation*}
      Hence, Voltmeter reads 5.985 V.
    2. For second figure [Subsection 2.3.4] No current flows. Hence, Ammeter reads 0 current and Voltmeter reads 6 V.
    3. For third figure No current flows. Hence, Ammeter reads 0 current and Voltmeter reads 6 V.
    Note: Ammeter is a negligibly low internal resistance device. It could not appreciably affect any current that is flowing in the sircuit. Hence it is always used as a series connection in the circuit.
    Voltmeter is an infinitely high internal resistance device. It could not allow any current flows through it from the circuit. Hence it is always used as a parallel connection in the circuit element at which we are measuring the voltage.

    Kirchhoff’s Law.

    Example 2.5.3.

    In the figure given below find
    1. the current through point a,
    2. the current through point b,
    3. the current through point c,
    4. the voltage at point d with respect to point c.
    Solution.
    Use loop currents direction as \(+ve.\)
    \begin{equation*} \text{(in loop i_1)} \quad 1 \,\Omega \, I_{1} + 2 \,\Omega \,I_{1} + 3 \,\Omega \,I_{1} - 3 \,\Omega \,I_{2} = 5\,V \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{(in loop i_2)} \quad 3\Omega \,I_{2} - 3 \,\Omega \,I_{1}+ 4 \,\Omega \,I_{2} + 5 \,\Omega \,I_{2} - 5 \,\Omega\,I_{3} = 10 \,V \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{(in loop i_3)} \quad 6\Omega \,I_{3} + 5 \,\Omega \,I_{3}- 5 \,\Omega \,I_{2} = -5 \,V \end{equation*}
    Simplified form of the above equations:
    \begin{equation} 6 \,I_{1} - 3\, I_{2} + 0\cdot \,I_{3} = 5 \tag{2.5.1} \end{equation}
    \begin{equation} - 3 \,I_{1} + 12 \,I_{2} - 5 \,I_{3} = 10\tag{2.5.2} \end{equation}
    \begin{equation} 0 \cdot \,I_{1} - 5 \,I_{2} + 11 \,I_{3} = - 5 \tag{2.5.3} \end{equation}
    In matrix form:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 6 & -3 & 0\\ -3 & 12 & -5\\ 0 & -5 & 11 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} I_{1}\\ I_{2}\\ I_{3} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5\\ 10\\ -5 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \begin{vmatrix} 6 & -3 & 0 : 5\\ -3 & 12 & -5 : 10\\ 0 & -5 & 11 : -5 \end{vmatrix} \end{equation*}
    The Determinant of the matrix:
    \begin{equation*} D =\begin{Vmatrix} 6 & -3 & 0\\ -3 & 12 & -5\\ 0 & -5 & 11 \end{Vmatrix} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} = 6[12\times 11 -(-5)(-5)] -(-3)[-3\times 11-0]+0= 543 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} I_{1}=\frac{\begin{Vmatrix} 5 & -3 & 0\\ 10 & 12 & -5\\ -5 & -5 & 11 \end{Vmatrix}}{D} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\frac{5[12\times11-(-5)(-5)]-(-3)[10\times11 -(-5)(-5)]+0}{D} = 1.45\,A \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} I_{2}=\frac{\begin{Vmatrix} 6 & 5 & 0\\ -3 & 10 & -5\\ 0 & -5 & 11 \end{Vmatrix}}{D} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\frac{6[10\times11-(-5)(-5)]-5[-3\times11 -0]+0}{D} = \frac{675}{543} = 1.24\,A \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} I_{3}=\frac{\begin{Vmatrix} 6 & -3 & 5\\ -3 & 12 & 10\\ 0 & -5 & -5 \end{Vmatrix}}{D} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\frac{6[12\times(-5)-(-5)(10)]-(-3)[-3\times(-5) -0]+5[(-3)\times(-5)-0]}{D} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore \quad I_{3}== \frac{60}{543} = 0.11\,A \end{equation*}
    1. \(\displaystyle I_{a} = I_{1} = 1.45\,A;\)
    2. \(\displaystyle I_{b} = I_{2} = 1.24\,A;\)
    3. \(\displaystyle I_{c} = I_{3} = 0.11\,A;\)
    4. \(V_{dc} = -[1I_{1} +2I_{1}]= -4.36\,V\)
      - ve sign is taken because current is going from point a to d.

    Example 2.5.4.

    A galvanometer is used as the meter in a multimeter. The galvanometer has a resistance of \(10 \,\Omega\) and reads full scale when \(1 \,mA\) is flowing through it.
    1. What is the minimum voltage that will cause the galvanometer to read full scale?
    2. What shunt resistance must be used in parallel with the galvanometer to make an ammeter that reads up to 0.1 A?
    3. What resistance must be placed in series with the galvanometer to make a voltmeter with a maximum range of 20 V?
    4. What is the voltage when the voltmeter reads one-quarter full scale?
    Solution.
    Given: \(g = 10 \,\Omega;\quad I_{g} = 1.00\times10^{-3} \,A;\) \(I_{b} = 0.1 \,A; \quad V_{c} = 20 \,V\)
    1. \begin{equation*} V_{a} = I_{a} \,g; \quad I_{a} = I_{g} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \therefore \quad V_{a}=10 \,mV \end{equation*}
    2. \begin{equation*} V_{b} =V_{a} = I_{b} R_{b}; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \quad R_{b} = 0.1\, \Omega; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{R_{b}}=\frac{1}{g}+\frac{1}{s} \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \therefore \quad s=0.1 \,\Omega \end{equation*}
    3. \begin{equation*} V_{c} = V_{g} R_{c}; \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} R_{c} = g+s=20 \,k\Omega \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation*} \therefore \quad s = 19.99 \,k\Omega \end{equation*}
    4. \begin{equation*} V_{d} = \frac{1}{4}V_{c}= 5 \,V \end{equation*}

    Example 2.5.5.

    The capacitor in the R-C circuit shown below contains no charge. The switch S is closed at time t = 0. If E = 6 V, \(C = 5 \,\mu F\) and \(R = 2 \,M\Omega,\) find
    1. the time constant of the circuit,
    2. the voltage across the resistor when t = 5 s,
    3. the charge on the capacitor when t = 5 s, and
    4. the current through the battery when t = 5 s.
    Solution.
    Given: \(E=6\,V; \quad C = 5.00\times10^{-6}\,F;\) \(R = 2.00\times10^{6}\,\Omega; \quad t =6\,s. \)
    \begin{equation*} E =\frac{q}{C}+IR; \quad I=\frac{\,dq}{\,dt};\quad V_{r} = IR \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} E- \frac{q}{C} =\frac{\,dq}{\,dt}R; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or,\quad \frac{E}{R}- \frac{q}{RC} =\frac{\,dq}{\,dt} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or,\quad \frac{EC-q}{RC} =\frac{\,dq}{\,dt}; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or, \quad \frac{\,dq}{EC-q} = \frac{\,dt}{RC} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or, \quad \int\limits_{0}^{Q}\frac{\,dq}{EC-q}=\int\limits_{0}^{t}\frac{\,dt}{RC}; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or, \quad \left.\frac{\ln(EC-q)}{-1}\right]_{0}^{Q} = \frac{t}{RC} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or, \quad \ln(EC-Q)-\ln(EC) =-\frac{t}{RC}; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or, \quad \ln\frac{EC-Q}{EC}=-\frac{t}{RC} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or, \quad EC-Q =ECe^{-\frac{t}{RC}}; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or, \quad Q = EC[1-e^{\frac{-t}{RC}}] \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or, \quad Q=Q_{o}[1-e^{\frac{-t}{\tau}}] \end{equation*}
    differentiate w.r.t ’t’, we get-
    \begin{equation*} I =I_{o}e^{-t/\tau} \end{equation*}
