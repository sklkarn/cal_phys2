    Subsection 2.4.3 Charging and Discharging of a Capacitor

    Charging and discharging of a capacitor circuit is shown in Figure 2.4.5.(a). Let us consider a capacitor (c) is completely discharged at \(t = 0\) when swich (k) is open. Now as the switch, k is closed to point 1, current begins to flow into the capacitor via resistor, R and capacitor begins charging gradually. Here resistor, R is needed in a circuit to slow down a charging or discharging process to protect capacitor.
    Applying Kirchhoff’s Voltage law to the charging loop, we have
    \begin{equation*} E=V_{R}+V_{c} = iR+\frac{q}{C}, \end{equation*}
    where \(E\) is emf of a battery, \(V_{R}\) is a potential drop at resistance \(R\text{,}\) \(V_{c}\) is a potential across the capacitor, \(C\) is capacitance of the capacitor, \(q\) is the charge stored in the capacitor at any time \(t,\) and \(i\) is a changing current in a circuit with time.
    \begin{equation*} E=\frac{\,dq}{\,dt}R+\frac{q}{C} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad E-\frac{q}{C}=\frac{\,dq}{\,dt}R \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \frac{EC-q}{RC}=\frac{\,dq}{\,dt} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \frac{\,dq}{EC-q}=\frac{\,dt}{RC} \end{equation*}
    Integrating, we get -
    \begin{equation*} \int\limits_{0}^{q}\frac{\,dq}{EC-q}=\int\limits_{0}^{t}\frac{\,dt}{RC} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \left.\frac{ln(EC-q)}{-1}\right\vert_{0}^{q}=\frac{t}{RC} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \ln(EC-q)-ln(EC)=-\frac{t}{RC} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad ln\left(\frac{EC-q}{EC}\right)=-\frac{t}{RC} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \frac{EC-q}{EC}=e^{(-\frac{t}{RC})} \end{equation*}
    Here \(EC=q_{o},\) is the maximum charge this capacitor can hold.
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \frac{q_{o}-q}{q_{o}}=e^{(-\frac{t}{RC})} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation} \therefore \quad q(t)= q_{o}\left[1-e^{(-\frac{t}{RC})}\right] \tag{2.4.8} \end{equation}
    This is the charge stored in a capacitor at any time t.
    Since, \(q(t)=CV_{c}(t)\) and \(q_{o}=EC\text{,}\) we have -
    \begin{equation} V_{c}(t)=E\left(1-e^{(-\frac{t}{RC})}\right)\tag{2.4.9} \end{equation}
    Now, differentiate eqn. (2.4.8) with respect to time t, we get-
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dq}{dt}=-q_{o}e^{(-\frac{t}{RC})}\left(-\frac{1}{RC}\right) = \frac{q_{o}}{RC}e^{(-\frac{t}{RC})} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation} \text{Hence} \quad I = I_{o}e^{-\frac{t}{RC}} \tag{2.4.10} \end{equation}
    where \(\frac{q_{o}}{RC}=I_{o},\) the maximum current in a circuit.
    When switch k is connected to the point 2, capacitor statrs discharging. Applying Kirchhoff’s voltage law to the discharging loop, we have -
    \begin{equation*} iR+\frac{q}{C}=0 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \frac{q}{C} = -iR=-\frac{\,dq}{\,dt}R \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \frac{\,dq}{q}=-\frac{\,dt}{RC} \end{equation*}
    Integrating, we get -
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \int\limits_{q_{o}}^{q(t)}\frac{\,dq}{q}=-\int\limits_{0}^{t}\frac{\,dt}{RC} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \left.ln q \right\vert_{q_{o}}^{q(t)} =-\frac{t}{RC} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad lnq(t)-lnq_{o}=-\frac{t}{RC} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad ln\frac{q(t)}{q_{o}}=-\frac{t}{RC} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \frac{q(t)}{q_{o}}=e^{-(\frac{t}{RC})} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation} \text{or,}\quad q(t) = q_{o} e^{-(\frac{t}{RC})} \tag{2.4.11} \end{equation}
    \begin{equation} \text{also,}\quad V_{c}(t)=Ee^{-(\frac{t}{RC})} \tag{2.4.12} \end{equation}
    Differentiating eqn. (2.4.11), we get-
    \begin{equation} i(t)=-i_{o}e^{-(\frac{t}{RC})} \tag{2.4.13} \end{equation}
    - ve sign indicates current is decreasing gradually during discharging of a capacitor. \(RC=\tau\) is called a time constant. The graph of charging and discharging of a capacitor is shown in Figure 2.4.5.(b). At one time constant potential of capacitors becomes \(63\%\) (emf of battery) during charging and \(36\%E\) during discharging.
