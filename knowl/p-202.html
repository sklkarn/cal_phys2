A cylindrical capacitor consists of two concentric conducting cylindrical shells of inner radius \(a\) and outer radius \(b\) [Figure 1.7.9] such that \(b \gt a\) and space between them is filled with dielectric of permittivity \(\epsilon_{o}\text{.}\) If a charge \(+Q\) is given to the inner shell then the charge \(-Q\) is induced on the inner surface of outer shell and charge \(+Q\) on the outer surface of outer shell. As the outer cylinder is grounded charge \(+Q\) neutralizes with the negative charge coming from the earth. Now using Gauss’s law the electric field at any point between the shells is given by
\begin{equation*} \phi = \int\vec{E}\cdot\vec{\,dA} = \frac{q_{enc}}{\epsilon_{o}} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \int E\,dA = E(2\pi rl) = \frac{\sigma 2\pi a l}{\epsilon_{o}} \end{equation*}
where \(r\) is the radius of Gaussian surface and \(\sigma\) is the surface charge density of the inner cylinder.
\begin{equation*} \therefore\quad E = \frac{\sigma a}{r \epsilon_{o}} \end{equation*}
The potential difference between the shells is then,
\begin{equation*} V=V_{b}-V_{a} = -\int\limits_{b}^{a}\vec{E}\cdot\vec{\,dr} = -\int\limits_{b}^{a}E\,dr \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad V= -\int\limits_{b}^{a}\frac{\sigma a}{r \epsilon_{o}}\,dr =-\frac{\sigma a}{\epsilon_{o}}\left(\ln r\right)_{b}^{a} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad V= \frac{\sigma a}{\epsilon_{o}}\left(\ln b-\ln a\right) = \frac{Q a}{2\pi a L \epsilon_{o}}\ln\frac{b}{a} \end{equation*}
Hence the capacitance of the cylindrical capacitor of length \(L\) is given by
\begin{equation*} C =\frac{Q}{V} = \frac{2\pi \epsilon_{o}L}{\ln\frac{b}{a}} \end{equation*}
