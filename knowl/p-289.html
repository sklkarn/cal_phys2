The resistivity of a conductor varies with temperature such that
\begin{equation*} \rho_{t} = \rho_{o}+at+bt^{2}+\cdots \end{equation*}
where \(\rho_{t},\quad \rho_{o} \) are resistivity at temperature \(t\) and \(0\,^{o}C\) and \(a,\,b,\,c\) are constants whose value depend on material. If temperature \(t\) is not sufficiently large then higher order terms are neglected hence,
\begin{equation*} \rho_{t} = \rho_{o}+at = \rho_{o}\left(1+\frac{a}{\rho_{o}}t\right)=\rho_{o}\left(1+\alpha t\right) \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \alpha = \frac{\rho_{t}-\rho_{o}}{\rho_{o}t} \end{equation*}
Here the constant \(\alpha\) is called temperature coefficient of resistivity of a conductor. It defines the material property. Now as resistance is proportional to the resistivity, we can write
\begin{equation} R = R_{o}[1 +\alpha(t-t_{o})] \tag{2.1.5} \end{equation}
i.e., a resistance or resistivity of metallic conductors increases with temperature.
