But for a thin prism, A is very small, i.e., \(A\simeq\tan A\text{.}\)
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \tan A=\tan\alpha+\tan\beta \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad A=\frac{y}{R_{1}}-\frac{y}{R_{2}} \end{equation*}
since radius of curvature for convex surface is +ve and that for concave surface is -ve. Considering light incident from left side of the lens.
\begin{equation} \text{or,}\quad \frac{A}{y}=\frac{1}{R_{1}}-\frac{1}{R_{2}} \tag{5.2.30} \end{equation}
If the light ray is incident at the same height of the lens it will be deviated by an angle \(\delta\text{,}\) where
\begin{equation*} \tan\delta=\frac{y}{f} \end{equation*}
[From FigureÂ 5.2.37.(a).]
\begin{equation*} \text{but,}\quad\delta=A(\eta-1) \end{equation*}
[for a prism.]
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \frac{y}{f}=A(\eta-1) \end{equation*}
\begin{equation} \text{or,}\quad \frac{1}{f}=\frac{A}{y}(\eta-1)\tag{5.2.31} \end{equation}
Therefore from eqns. (5.2.30) and (5.2.31), we get -
\begin{equation} \frac{1}{f}= (\eta - 1)\left(\frac{1}{R_{1}} - \frac{1}{R_{2}}\right) \tag{5.2.32} \end{equation}
Similar relation can also be obtained for a concave lens.
