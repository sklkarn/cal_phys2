  1. Point \(P\) lies outside the charge distribution, ie., \(r \gt R\) as shwon in Figure 1.4.3.(a).
    \begin{equation*} \phi=\oint\vec{E}\cdot\vec{\,ds}= E_{o}4\pi r^{2} =\frac{Q_{enc}}{\epsilon_{o}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad E_{o} = \left(\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_{o}}\right) \frac{Q}{r^{2}}= k\frac{Q}{r^{2}} \end{equation*}
    Hence the electric field at any point outside the spherical charge distribution is same as though the entire charge is concentrated at the center.
  2. Point \(P\) lies on the surface of charge distribution, ie., \(r = R.\)
    \begin{equation*} E_{s} = k\frac{Q}{R^{2}} \end{equation*}
  3. Point \(P\) lies inside the charge distribution, ie., \(r \lt R\) as shown in Figure 1.4.3.(b).
    \begin{equation*} \text{charge density,}\quad \rho= \frac{Q}{V_{sphere}}=\frac{Q}{\frac{4}{3}\pi R^{3}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \oint\vec{E_{i}}\cdot\vec{\,ds}= \frac{Q_{enc}}{\epsilon_{o}} = \frac{1}{\epsilon_{o}}\int\rho\,dV \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} E_{i}(4\pi r^{2})= \frac{1}{\epsilon_{o}}\rho (\frac{4}{3}\pi r^{3}) = \frac{1}{\epsilon_{o}}(\frac{Q}{\frac{4}{3}\pi R^{3}}) (\frac{4}{3}\pi r^{3}) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad E_{i} = \frac{1}{4\pi \epsilon_{o}}\frac{Q r}{R^{3}} = k \frac{Q r}{R^{3}} \end{equation*}
    Thus the electric field inside a spherical charge distribution is directly proportional to the distance of the point from the center of spherical charge. The variation of field with the distance from the center of spherical charge distribution is shown in Figure 1.4.3.(c).
