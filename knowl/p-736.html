When two or more waves superimpose (overlap) at a point then the resultant displacement of the medium particle is the algebraic sum of the displacement of the particle due to individual waves. This is known as principle of superposition. Let \(y_{1}(x,t)\) and \(y_{2}(x,t)\) are the wave functions of two waves, then from priciple of superposition the resulting displacement of a particle at that point is given by the wave function
\begin{equation*} y(x,t) = y_{1}(x,t)+y_{2}(x,t) \end{equation*}
Wave function has a linear property (meaning wave function depends only the first power of x and t but not on their higher power) hence the principle of superposition holds good only in a medium which obeys Hookeâ€™s Law. Superposition of two or more waves gives rise to interference. When two similar waves superimpose in the same phase their amplitude re-enforce each other and resultant amplitude is just the double of their individual amplitude such interference is called a constructive interference. For constructive interference the path difference between two superimposing waves is given by
\begin{equation*} \Delta x = n \lambda, \quad n = 0, 1, 2,\cdots \end{equation*}
When two similar waves superimpose in the opposite phase their amplitude cancels out each other and resultant amplitude is just zero such interference is called a destructive interference. For destructive interference the path difference between two superimposing waves is given by
\begin{equation*} \Delta x = \frac{n}{2} \lambda, \quad n = 1, 3, 5,\cdots \end{equation*}
The path difference of two waves is related to their phase difference by
\begin{equation*} \Delta \theta = 2\pi \left(\frac{\Delta x}{\lambda}\right) \end{equation*}
