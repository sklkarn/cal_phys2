  1. Interference maxima and minima: Consider the secondary waves traveling in direction \(\theta\) with the initial direction of the incident light. Now from \(\triangle ACN\) from FigureÂ 5.8.13
    \begin{equation*} \sin\theta=\frac{CN}{AC}=\frac{CN}{a+b}\quad \Rightarrow CN=(a+b)\sin\theta \end{equation*}
    If the path difference CN is an odd multiple of \(\lambda/2\text{,}\) then \(\theta\) gives the direction of minima due to interference of the secondary waves.
    \begin{equation} \therefore\quad CN = (a+b) \sin\theta_{n}=(2n+1)\frac{\lambda}{2} \quad [n=1,2,3,\cdots] \tag{5.8.20} \end{equation}
    \begin{equation} \text{or,}\quad \sin\theta_{n}=\frac{(2n+1)\lambda}{2(a+b)} \tag{5.8.21} \end{equation}
    But when CN is an even multiple of \(\lambda/2\text{,}\) then \(\theta\) gives the direction of maxima due to interference of the secondary waves.
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad CN = (a+b) \sin\theta_{n}=2n\frac{\lambda}{2} \quad [n=1,2,3,\cdots] \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation} \text{or,}\quad \sin\theta_{n}=\frac{n\lambda}{a+b} \tag{5.8.22} \end{equation}
    The angular separation between any two consecutive minima or maxima is given by
    \begin{equation*} \sin\theta_{n}-\sin\theta_{n-1} = \frac{\lambda}{a+b} \end{equation*}
  2. Diffraction maxima and minima: Consider the secondary waves traveling in direction \(\phi\) with the initial direction of the incident light from a same slit. If the path difference BM is equal to \(\lambda\text{,}\) then \(\phi \) will give the direction of diffraction minimum. If the wavefront AB divides into two halves, then the path difference between the corresponding points of the upper and lower half is equal to \(\lambda/2.\) Hence the point P has no effect due to wavefront coming from slit AB. Similarly, there is no effect at P due to the waves coming from slit CD with the same inclination. In general,
    \begin{equation} a\sin\phi_{n} = n\lambda \quad [n=1,2,3,\cdots] \tag{5.8.23} \end{equation}
