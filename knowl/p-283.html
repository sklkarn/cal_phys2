Consider a conductor of length \(L\text{,}\) area of cross-section \(A\text{,}\) is subjected to an electric field \(E\) due to which a charge \(\,dq\) crosses one section of a conductor [Figure 2.1.1] in time \(\,dt\text{,}\) then current
\begin{equation*} I = \frac{\,dq}{\,dt} \end{equation*}
If there are \(N\) number of charge carriers cross through a section of the conductor, then
\begin{equation*} I= \frac{\,d(N e)}{\,dt} \end{equation*}
where \(e\) is the charge of each charge carrier (or an electron). Hence,
\begin{align*} I\amp = \frac{\,dq}{\,dt} \\ \amp = \frac{\,d(N e)}{\,dt}\\ \amp = e\,\frac{\,dN }{\,dt} \\ \amp =e \,\frac{n \,dV}{\,dt} \\ \amp =e\frac{nA\,dx}{\,dt}\\ \amp =n e v_{d}A \end{align*}
\begin{equation} I = n e v A\tag{2.1.1} \end{equation}
where \(A\) is area of cross-section of the conductor, \(n\) is the number density (\(n=\frac{\,dN}{\,dV},\) ) and \(v_{d}=v\) is a drift velocity of electrons.  1
