Applying Kirchhoffâ€™s Voltage law to the charging loop, we have
\begin{equation*} E=V_{R}+V_{c} = iR+\frac{q}{C}, \end{equation*}
where \(E\) is emf of a battery, \(V_{R}\) is a potential drop at resistance \(R\text{,}\) \(V_{c}\) is a potential across the capacitor, \(C\) is capacitance of the capacitor, \(q\) is the charge stored in the capacitor at any time \(t,\) and \(i\) is a changing current in a circuit with time.
\begin{equation*} E=\frac{\,dq}{\,dt}R+\frac{q}{C} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad E-\frac{q}{C}=\frac{\,dq}{\,dt}R \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \frac{EC-q}{RC}=\frac{\,dq}{\,dt} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \frac{\,dq}{EC-q}=\frac{\,dt}{RC} \end{equation*}
Integrating, we get -
\begin{equation*} \int\limits_{0}^{q}\frac{\,dq}{EC-q}=\int\limits_{0}^{t}\frac{\,dt}{RC} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \left.\frac{ln(EC-q)}{-1}\right\vert_{0}^{q}=\frac{t}{RC} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \ln(EC-q)-ln(EC)=-\frac{t}{RC} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad ln\left(\frac{EC-q}{EC}\right)=-\frac{t}{RC} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \frac{EC-q}{EC}=e^{(-\frac{t}{RC})} \end{equation*}
Here \(EC=q_{o},\) is the maximum charge this capacitor can hold.
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \frac{q_{o}-q}{q_{o}}=e^{(-\frac{t}{RC})} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation} \therefore \quad q(t)= q_{o}\left[1-e^{(-\frac{t}{RC})}\right] \tag{2.4.8} \end{equation}
This is the charge stored in a capacitor at any time t.
