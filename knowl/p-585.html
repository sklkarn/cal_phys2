It is an effective value of alternating current which develops the same amount of heat in resistance R as direct current (or steady current) will develop when flowing through it. That is,
\begin{equation*} I^{2}_{eff}R=I^{2}R= \overline{I}^{2}R \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \therefore\quad I_{eff}=I_{rms} = \sqrt{\overline{I}^{2}} \end{equation*}
Thus the rms value of current is the square root of mean of squares of instantaneous values over one complete cycle. If an alternating current is represented by
\begin{equation*} I=I_{o}\sin\omega t \end{equation*}
where \(I_{o}\) is an amplitude of the alternating current, then mean square current is
\begin{equation*} \overline{I}^{2} = \frac{\int\limits_{0}^{T}I^{2}\,dt}{\int\limits_{0}^{T}\,dt} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} = \frac{1}{T} \int\limits_{0}^{T} I_{o}^{2}\sin^{2}\omega t\,dt = \frac{I^{2}_{o}}{2T}\int\limits_{0}^{T} 2\sin^{2}\omega t\,dt \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} = \frac{I^{2}_{o}}{2T}\int\limits_{0}^{T}(1- \cos 2\omega t)\,dt = \frac{I^{2}_{o}}{2T}\left(t-\frac{\sin 2\omega t}{2\omega}\right)_{0}^{T} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} = \frac{I^{2}_{o}}{2T} T = \frac{I^{2}_{o}}{2} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \therefore\quad I_{rms}= \sqrt{\overline{I}^{2}} = \frac{I_{o}}{\sqrt{2}} = 0.707 I_{o} \end{equation*}
similarly,
\begin{equation*} E_{rms}= \sqrt{\overline{E}^{2}} = \frac{E_{o}}{\sqrt{2}} = 0.707 E_{o} \end{equation*}
The hot wire meter is used to measure rms value of current. It measures the expansion of wire due to heat generated as current flow in it.
\begin{equation*} \textbf{Form Factor}= \frac{I_{rms}}{I_{av}} = \frac{E_{rms}}{E_{av}} = \frac{\pi}{2\sqrt{2}}=1.11 \end{equation*}
