When wave passes through the medium it transfers energy to that medium. Consider a small portion of oscillating string in which sinusoidal wave is propagated then average energy attains by the particle of a string is given by
\begin{equation*} E_{avg} =\frac{1}{2}\,m v^{2}_{max}=\frac{1}{2}\mu \,dx (A\omega)^{2} \end{equation*}
where m and \(v_{max}\) is the mass and maximum velocity of the particle of that string.
\begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad P_{avg} =\frac{\,dE}{\,dt}= \frac{1}{2}\mu v A^{2}\omega^{2} =\frac{1}{2}\mu \sqrt{\frac{T}{\mu}} A^{2}\omega^{2} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \therefore \quad P_{avg} = \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\mu T} \omega^{2}A^{2} \quad \text{(In String)} \end{equation*}
Waves on a string carry energy along the length of the string, but when waves pass through a solid or a fluid they carry energy along all three dimensions of space. Hence we need to define intensity of wave, which is energy transmitted by the wave in unit area of this material in unit time.
\begin{equation*} I = \frac{E}{A t} = \frac{\frac{1}{2}mv^{2}_{max}} {A t} =\frac{\frac{1}{2}\rho V (s_{o}\omega)^{2}} {A t} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \therefore \quad I =\frac{1}{2} \rho v s^{2}_{o}\omega^{2} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation} I \propto A^2 \quad [\because \quad s_o=A.]\tag{4.4.6} \end{equation}
\begin{equation*} =\frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\rho B} \omega^{2} s^{2}_{o}\quad \text{(In Fluid)} \end{equation*}
here \(V=A.x\text{,}\) \(s_{o}\) is amplitude of oscillation, and v is wave speed.
\begin{equation*} I = \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\rho Y} \omega^{2}s^{2}_{o} \quad \text{(In Solid)} \end{equation*}
Since intensity obeys inverse square law as
\begin{equation*} I=\frac{P}{A} = \frac{P}{4\pi r^{2}} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} or, I\propto \frac{1}{r^{2}} \end{equation*}
where \(4\pi r^{2}\) is the surface area of a sphere of radius, r of space in which wave is propagated. Hence intensity of the wave decreases by the square of the distance it propagated.
