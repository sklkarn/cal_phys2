    Subsection 1.7.1 Capacitors

    In any electronic circuit we can see some of these types of devices [Figure¬†1.7.4]. They are known as condenser or capacitor.
    Capacitor: It is a charge storing device. It can store electrical energy in the form of electrical charge that produces potential difference across its plates. Since we can also store charge on a conductor it can also be considered as capacitor but it can store a very limited amount of charge for a short duration only. A capacity of storing charge on a conductor can be increased by arranging two or more parallel conducting surfaces that are placed close to each other with insulating material/s between them, e.g. air and/or any dielectrics. If these surfaces are plane we call it a parallel plate capacitor, if they are cylindrical it is a cylindrical capacitor, and if they are spherical it is a spherical capacitor.
    Capacitance: The capacity of storing charge on a capacitor is termed as capacitance. when an isolated conductor is given a charge it acquires a certain potential. If is found that the potential V acquired by the conductor is proportional to the charge Q given to it i.e.
    \begin{equation*} Q \propto V \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad Q = CV; \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad C = \frac{Q}{V}. \end{equation*}
    Here C is a proportionality constant known as capacitance of that conductor (or capacitor). Hence capacitance is defined as the charge required to raise the potential of the conductor by unity. Its unit is Farad. If one coulomb of charge stored in a capacitor of potential difference across the plates is one volt then its capacitance is one Farad. Farad is a very large number. The entire earth as a spherical capacitor has a capacitance of only 0.0007 Farad.
    The increase of capacitance of a conductor can be understood by the following arrangement. Let A be an isolated conductor which is charged to a maximum potential V volts. [Note: Potential refers to a potential difference of a conductor with respect to ground.]
    Suppose we want to store some more charge on it then we have to reduce its potential somehow before putting any charge. So another isolated conductor B is brought near A then A induces negative charges on the inner surface of B and positive charges on the outer surface as shown in Figure¬†1.7.5.(a). The negative charge on B tends to reduce the potential of A and positive charge on B tends to increase potential of A. Since negative charges of B are closer to A than the positive charges of B the net potential of A is reduced by some amount. This slight decrees in potential of A results increase of capacitance of A because of relation
    \begin{equation*} C = \frac{Q}{V} \end{equation*}
    remaining Q constant. Hence a more charge can be stored on the same conductor till the potential again reaches to V. The capacitance can be considerably high if the conductors are placed very close to each other and a conductor B is grounded (Figure¬†1.7.5.(b)). The relation
    \begin{equation*} V = k\frac{Q}{r}= \frac{1}{4\pi \epsilon}\frac{Q}{r}, \end{equation*}
    explains that the increase in permitivity of the dielectric medium between conducting plates also increases the capacitance.
    Absolute Permittivity, \(\epsilon\text{:}\) [Subsection¬†1.1.2] It is a permittivity of a medium and defines how difficult it is for a medium to form an electric field.
    \begin{equation*} \epsilon =\kappa\epsilon_{o} \end{equation*}

    Subsubsection 1.7.1.1 Combination of Capacitors

    The two or more than two capacitors can be grouped in an electrical circuit to achieve certain task. These group may be series or parallel or in combination of both.
    1. Series Combination of Capacitors When capacitors are connected in such a way that same charge is seemed to be passing through each capacitor then the combination is called a series combination of capacitors. In this case positive terminal of one capacitor is connected to the negative terminal of another capacitor as shown in Figure¬†1.7.6.(a). If charge Q is sent in the circuit by a battery of emf, V [Section¬†2.1], then
      \begin{equation*} V=V_{1} +V_{2}= \frac{Q}{C_{1}}+\frac{Q}{C_{2}} = Q\left[\frac{1}{C_{1}}+\frac{1}{C_{2}}\right] \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation} \frac{V}{Q}= \left[\frac{1}{C_{1}}+\frac{1}{C_{2}}\right]\tag{1.7.1} \end{equation}
      Now replace these capacitors with such a single capacitor that draws a same amount of charge Q from the same battery as shown in Figure¬†1.7.6.(c), then this new capacitor is called an equivalent capacitor \(C_{eq}\) of all these previous capacitors in series.
      \begin{equation} V= \frac{Q}{C_{eq}};\quad \text{or,}\quad \frac{V}{Q}=\frac{1}{C_{eq}} \tag{1.7.2} \end{equation}
      Hence, from eqns. (1.7.1) and (1.7.2), we get -
      \begin{equation} \frac{1}{C_{eq}}= \frac{1}{C_{1}}+ \frac{1}{C_{2}} \tag{1.7.3} \end{equation}
    2. Parallel Combination of Capacitors When capacitors are connected in such a way that charges are distributed among capacitors then the combination is called a parallel combination of capacitors. In this case similar terminals of capacitors are connected together as shown in Figure¬†1.7.6.(b). If q is the charge sent in the circuit by a battery of emf, V then
      \begin{equation*} q=q_{1}+q_{2}=C_{1}V+C_{2}V = V\left[C_{1}+C_{2}\right] \end{equation*}
      \begin{equation} \therefore\quad \frac{q}{V} = \left[C_{1}+C_{2}\right] \tag{1.7.4} \end{equation}
      Now if these capacitors are replaced by a single capacitor, \(C_{eq}\) such that circuit draws the same amount of charge from the same battery (Figure¬†1.7.6.(c)), then
      \begin{equation} q = C_{eq}V \quad \text{or,}\quad \frac{q}{V}=C_{eq} \tag{1.7.5} \end{equation}
      Hence from eqns. (1.7.4) and (1.7.5), we get -
      \begin{equation} C_{eq} = C_{1} + C_{2} \tag{1.7.6} \end{equation}

    Subsubsection 1.7.1.2 Parallel-Plate Capacitor

    A parallel plate capacitor [Figure¬†1.7.7] consists of two metal plates of large surface area placed close to each other parallely with one of the plates grounded. Let \(A\) be the surface area of the plates and they are separated by a distance \(d\) apart. If surface charge density of the plates are \(\sigma\) and space between the plates is filled with dielectric of permittivity \(\epsilon_{o}.\) Then the electric field between the plates is given by
    \begin{equation*} E=E_{1}+E_{2}= \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_{o}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{where,}\quad E_{1}= \frac{\sigma}{2\epsilon_{o}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{and}\quad E_{2}= \frac{\sigma}{2\epsilon_{o}} \end{equation*}
    pointed along -ve plate.
    \begin{equation*} \text{But,}\quad V = -\int\vec{E}\cdot\vec{\,dr} = Ed =\frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_{o}}d \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{and}\quad \sigma = \frac{Q}{A} \end{equation*}
    where \(V\) is the potential difference between the plates and the angle between \(\vec{\,dr}\) and \(\vec{E}\) is \(180^{o}.\)
    \begin{equation*} C_{o} = \frac{Q}{V}= \frac{\sigma A}{Ed} = \frac{\sigma A}{\frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_{o}}d}=\frac{A \epsilon_{o}}{d} \end{equation*}
    If the space between the plates is filled with dielectric with permitivity \(\epsilon\) then capacitance of a parallel plate capacitor is given by
    \begin{equation} C=\frac{A \epsilon}{d}\tag{1.7.7} \end{equation}
    Dielectric constant, \(\kappa\) (or Relative Permitivity, \(\epsilon_{r}\)): It is a measure of insulating ability of a medium. It is a ratio of the permitivity of the medium \((\epsilon)\) to the permitivity of a free space \((\epsilon_{o})\) i.e.,
    \begin{equation*} \kappa=\epsilon_{r} =\frac{\epsilon}{\epsilon_{o}}. \end{equation*}
    It is an ability of a medium to store electrical energy in presence of electric field.
    \begin{equation} \kappa = \frac{\epsilon}{\epsilon_{o}}= \frac{C}{C_{o}}\tag{1.7.8} \end{equation}

    Subsubsection 1.7.1.3 Spherical Capacitor

    A spherical capacitor consists of two concentric conducting spherical shells [Figure¬†1.7.8] of inner radius \(a\) and outer radius \(b\) such that \(b \gt a\) and space between them is filled with dielectric of permittivity \(\epsilon_{o}\text{.}\) If a charge \(+Q\) is given to the inner shell then \(-Q\) amount of charge is induced on the inner surface of outer shell and charge \(+Q\) on the outer surface of outer shell. As the outer surface is grounded charge \(+Q\) neutralizes with the negative charge coming from the earth. Now using Gauss‚Äôs law the electric field at any point between the shells is given
    \begin{equation*} \phi = \int\vec{E}\cdot\vec{\,dA} = \frac{q_{enc}}{\epsilon_{o}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \int E\,dA = E(4\pi r^{2}) = \frac{Q}{\epsilon_{o}} \end{equation*}
    where \(r\) is the radius of Gaussian surface and \(q\) is the charge enclosed by it.
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad E = \frac{Q}{4\pi \epsilon_{o}r^{2}}=\frac{kQ}{r^{2}} \end{equation*}
    The potential difference between the shells is then,
    \begin{equation*} V=V_{b}-V_{a} = -\int\limits_{b}^{a}\vec{E}\cdot\vec{\,dr} = -\int\limits_{b}^{a}E\,dr \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} or, \quad V = -\int\limits_{b}^{a}\frac{kQ}{r^{2}}\,dr = kQ\left(\frac{1}{r}\right)_{b}^{a}= kQ\left(\frac{1}{a}-\frac{1}{b}\right) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \frac{V}{Q} = k\left(\frac{b-a}{ab}\right) \end{equation*}
    Hence the capacitance of the spherical capacitor is given by
    \begin{equation*} C=\frac{Q}{V} = \frac{1}{k}\left(\frac{ab}{b-a}\right)= 4\pi\epsilon_{o}\left(\frac{ab}{b-a}\right) \end{equation*}
    If \(b \rightarrow\infty\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} C=4\pi\epsilon_{o}\left(\frac{a}{1-\frac{a}{b}}\right)= 4\pi\epsilon_{o} a \end{equation*}
    Which is the capacitance of an isolated spherical conductor of radius \(a\text{.}\)

    Subsubsection 1.7.1.4 Cylindrical Capacitor

    A cylindrical capacitor consists of two concentric conducting cylindrical shells of inner radius \(a\) and outer radius \(b\) [Figure¬†1.7.9] such that \(b \gt a\) and space between them is filled with dielectric of permittivity \(\epsilon_{o}\text{.}\) If a charge \(+Q\) is given to the inner shell then the charge \(-Q\) is induced on the inner surface of outer shell and charge \(+Q\) on the outer surface of outer shell. As the outer cylinder is grounded charge \(+Q\) neutralizes with the negative charge coming from the earth. Now using Gauss‚Äôs law the electric field at any point between the shells is given by
    \begin{equation*} \phi = \int\vec{E}\cdot\vec{\,dA} = \frac{q_{enc}}{\epsilon_{o}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad \int E\,dA = E(2\pi rl) = \frac{\sigma 2\pi a l}{\epsilon_{o}} \end{equation*}
    where \(r\) is the radius of Gaussian surface and \(\sigma\) is the surface charge density of the inner cylinder.
    \begin{equation*} \therefore\quad E = \frac{\sigma a}{r \epsilon_{o}} \end{equation*}
    The potential difference between the shells is then,
    \begin{equation*} V=V_{b}-V_{a} = -\int\limits_{b}^{a}\vec{E}\cdot\vec{\,dr} = -\int\limits_{b}^{a}E\,dr \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad V= -\int\limits_{b}^{a}\frac{\sigma a}{r \epsilon_{o}}\,dr =-\frac{\sigma a}{\epsilon_{o}}\left(\ln r\right)_{b}^{a} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{or,}\quad V= \frac{\sigma a}{\epsilon_{o}}\left(\ln b-\ln a\right) = \frac{Q a}{2\pi a L \epsilon_{o}}\ln\frac{b}{a} \end{equation*}
    Hence the capacitance of the cylindrical capacitor of length \(L\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} C =\frac{Q}{V} = \frac{2\pi \epsilon_{o}L}{\ln\frac{b}{a}} \end{equation*}
